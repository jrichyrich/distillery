## Summary

The front matter of *From Heatmaps to Histograms* establishes the book's scope, authorship, and intellectual lineage. Tony Martin-Vegue, a practitioner with over 25 years of experience and an estimated 1,000 quantitative risk assessments under his belt, positions the book as a practical, hands-on guide for cybersecurity professionals who want to move from qualitative color-coded risk methods to quantitative measurement. The book is published by Apress (Springer) and carries endorsements from the foundational figures in cyber risk quantification: Jack Jones (creator of FAIR), Douglas Hubbard (author of *How to Measure Anything*), Richard Seiersen, Wade Baker, Jay Jacobs, Lisa Young, and Rick Howard.

The table of contents reveals a five-part structure that mirrors a learning journey: Part I (Foundations) makes the case for CRQ and addresses GenAI; Part II (Getting Your Risk Muscles Working) covers Monte Carlo simulation, interpretation, and risk statements; Part III (Solving the Data Problem) tackles external data, internal data, SME elicitation, and Bayesian blending; Part IV (Risk Assessment in Action) walks through complete assessments using FAIR; and Part V (Making It Stick) focuses on organizational adoption and the future of CRQ. The book requires only Microsoft Excel, access to basic research sources, and a willingness to think probabilistically.

Martin-Vegue's acknowledgments reveal the intellectual debt to Jones, Hubbard, and Seiersen, framing the book as an extension of their foundational work into broader contexts, methods, and decision types. The technical reviewer, Robert D. Brown III, brings three decades of decision and risk analysis experience across industries including cybersecurity and insurance.

## Key Points

- The book targets beginners but includes intermediate and advanced techniques for practitioners at any stage
- It is model-agnostic and FAIR-compatible, not exclusively a FAIR methodology book
- Five-part structure progresses from foundations through hands-on modeling to organizational embedding
- Only requires Microsoft Excel -- no specialized software needed
- GenAI tools are recommended but not required; prompts are provided throughout
- The book covers risk analysis specifically, not the entire risk management lifecycle
- Endorsed by the core luminaries of the CRQ field: Jack Jones, Douglas Hubbard, Richard Seiersen, Wade Baker, Jay Jacobs
- Robert D. Brown III served as technical reviewer, bringing cross-industry risk analysis expertise

## Core Principles

- **Practitioner-first approach**: Battle-tested methods from real-world assessments, not academic theory
- **Model agnosticism**: Techniques work across FAIR, custom models, or even within existing qualitative programs
- **Progressive learning**: Chapters build like Lego blocks, each concept scaffolding the next
- **Accessibility**: Designed for the broadest possible audience; no advanced math required

## Practical Takeaways

- Start at the beginning if new to quantitative risk; skip to Part II or III if you have some background
- Use the companion website (heatmapstohistograms.com) for downloads, tools, templates, and errata
- Do not skip the exercises -- the concepts stick better through practice with real scenarios
- Explore the recommended reading list from the endorsers for deeper dives into specific areas

## Key Terms & Concepts

- **[[Cyber Risk Quantification]]** (CRQ): The practice of expressing risk in numbers (ranges of possible losses) instead of colors or adjectives
- **[[FAIR]]** (Factor Analysis of Information Risk): The most widely adopted quantitative risk framework in cybersecurity, created by Jack Jones
- **[[Monte Carlo Simulation]]**: A method using randomness within ranges to simulate thousands of likely outcomes
- **[[Risk Analysis]]**: The measurement part of risk -- understanding frequency and magnitude
- **[[Risk Assessment]]**: The complete business process including analysis, scoping, and decision support
