## Summary

The final chapter of *From Heatmaps to Histograms* looks beyond models and methods to the future of the risk analyst profession. Martin-Vegue traces the book's arc from heatmaps to histograms and now forward into an AI-transformed landscape. He cites *The New School of Information Security* by Adam Shostack and Andrew Stewart (2008) as the personal epiphany that showed him "the real gap in security wasn't technical. It was intellectual." Nearly two decades later, he argues the dashboards got brighter but the reasoning didn't keep up -- and CRQ was the bridge from gut feel to good data, while AI is "the bridge from data to insight at scale."

The chapter's most urgent message concerns the AI-driven skill shift. Martin-Vegue draws a line between automatable skills (data collection, routine calculations, compliance cross-mapping, summarization, basic visualizations) and uniquely human differentiators (critical thinking, strategic framing, risk judgment, communication and influence, ethical reasoning). He places prompt engineering in a transitional zone -- valuable now but fading as AI improves at generating its own prompts. His advice: "If most of your work sits in the red boxes, you need to start shifting toward the green ones." He frames the AI revolution with a striking analogy: "If the Industrial Revolution began around 1760, think about what it must have been like to be a farmer in 1761. Those farmers didn't know what was coming."

Adaptation must happen at three levels: organizational (leaders invest in people and set AI guardrails), team (standardize safe workflows, pair AI with peer review, share experiments in a knowledge base), and individual. The individual section is the part Martin-Vegue says he would want readers to remember "even if the rest of the book ends up as fireplace kindling." He advocates building a "home CRQ lab" -- a fictitious company with synthetic data generated by AI -- to practice quantification without waiting for employer permission. He shares that he still does this himself, running a local LLM on an air-gapped Mac Mini to model a midsize US financial institution. The chapter closes with a call to teach, mentor, publish, and share: "The field will grow in proportion to how generously we share what we know." The final line: "Welcome to the rebellion -- the rational one."

## Key Points

- Shostack and Stewart's *The New School of Information Security* (2008) was Martin-Vegue's career inflection point -- it showed that security's gap was intellectual, not technical
- "CRQ was the bridge from gut feel to good data. AI is the bridge from data to insight at scale."
- The risk analyst profession is undergoing "a seismic realignment" -- some tasks will disappear, others evolve, and new ones will appear
- The farmer-in-1761 analogy: most revolutions feel invisible when you're living through them, but this time we can see what's coming
- AI excels at automatable tasks: data collection/preprocessing, routine calculations, compliance checks, framework cross-mapping, summarization, basic visualization
- Human differentiators that AI cannot replace: critical thinking, strategic framing and decision context, risk judgment and prioritization, communication and influence, ethical reasoning and governance
- Prompt engineering is a transitional skill -- valuable now but will be automated; the lasting advantage goes to those who can ask good questions
- "This is the only time I'll ever tell you to turn reds into greens" -- a self-aware callback to the book's critique of heatmaps
- Job security comes to analysts who are "deliberate about their growth, who double down on the skills that machines cannot replace"
- Don't wait for your employer, university, or manager to tell you what to learn -- own your growth
- The real progress in CRQ is cultural, not technological: "some organizations are beginning to think probabilistically"
- "The work ahead is not to protect data, but to protect reasoning"
- "The world does not need another security risk framework. It needs people who can reason about uncertainty"

## Core Principles

- **The Skill Shift Framework**: A two-zone model dividing risk analyst skills into automatable (red zone: data tasks, calculations, compliance mapping) and human differentiators (green zone: judgment, framing, communication, ethics), with prompt engineering as a transitional skill between them
- **Three Levels of Adaptation**: Organizational (set vision, build guardrails, invest in people), Team (experiment together, standardize workflows, pair AI with peer review), Individual (own your growth, build a home lab, practice continuously)
- **The Home CRQ Lab**: A five-step personal practice environment: (1) create a fictitious organization, (2) define business context, (3) generate synthetic data with AI, (4) analyze and model, (5) reflect and refine
- **The Bridge Metaphor**: CRQ bridges gut feel to good data; AI bridges data to insight at scale -- both extend the same trajectory toward rational decision-making
- **Protect Reasoning, Not Just Data**: The ultimate purpose of the profession is not technological defense but preserving the capacity for clear, evidence-based thinking

## Practical Takeaways

- Audit your current skill portfolio against the Skill Shift framework: if most of your daily work is in the automatable zone, prioritize developing human differentiator skills
- Build a home CRQ lab using the five-step method: pick a fictitious company (bank, SaaS, hospital), define its business context, use AI to generate synthetic incidents/audits/telemetry, then practice frequency modeling, loss analysis, and Monte Carlo simulation
- Use the provided GenAI prompt template to bootstrap a home lab environment quickly
- Consider running a local LLM on an air-gapped machine (as Martin-Vegue does) for safe experimentation without data leakage risk
- Don't wait for organizational permission to develop CRQ skills -- "When your employer can't move fast enough, your home lab becomes your accelerator"
- At the team level, document AI experiments (not just outcomes) in a shared knowledge base; treat AI as input to decisions, not the decision itself
- At the organizational level, advocate for AI guardrails, retraining budgets, and policies enabling safe experimentation
- Teach, mentor, publish, and share your models -- the field grows through generosity of knowledge
- Blend home lab synthetic datasets with external data (Chapter 11) and SME ranges (Chapter 13) to simulate real-world uncertainty
