## The Antipattern

Tin Man organizations believe that optimizing today is safer than inventing for an uncertain tomorrow, so they focus on short-term gains and incremental improvements as risk-reducing strategies. The results are increased risk of disruption and erosion of market position, mediocre performance, and missed opportunities to redefine markets.

## Why It Happens

Organizations like Intel -- or Blockbuster, Toys "R" Us, Sears, or even Xerox PARC -- could not see the future. The tragedy is that many of them saw it, even invented it, but still chose to focus on the now. Intel focused intensely on faster, more powerful chips for profitable customers while competitors captured the exploding smartphone and tablet market. Inside, rationalizations sounded like: "Mobile chips aren't profitable enough" or "Our enterprise customers demand maximum performance."

Several forces prevent bigger thinking:

- **Cost**: Markets and investors pressure short-term earnings targets and rigid ROI requirements
- **Leadership**: Risk-averse executives protect tenure and compensation tied to quarterly results. As Bezos observed: "We tend to overestimate risk and underestimate opportunity so that thinking small becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy"
- **Inertia**: Success breeds complacency; product cannibalization is frowned upon
- **Cognition**: Leaders struggle to imagine radical change because it feels uncomfortable
- **Structure**: Siloed departments, legacy systems, technical debt, and bureaucratic processes limit agility

Astro Teller of Alphabet X poses a stark choice: give $1 million of value to your business this year guaranteed (Choice A), or give $1 billion of value with a one-in-one-hundred chance (Choice B). Most select Choice B with a confident smile. When asked if their managers would support Choice B, they inevitably answer "no." Astro's response: "You don't need a lecture on innovation. You need a new manager."

Even when desire exists, executives are overworked and exhausted with no energy left for the future. Given decreasing C-level tenure, there is simply no incentive for ambitious long-term plans.

## The Octopus Way

**Focus on Gain, Not Risk.** Instead of calculating downside risks and protecting existing assets, Octopus Organizations become energized by upside potential and future opportunities. This shift from loss aversion to gain orientation changes decision-making to be faster, bolder, and more innovative.

**Think Big.** Big ideas share key characteristics: ambitious vision that seems impossible, contrarian challenges to status quo, radical solutions leveraging new technologies, transformative exponential impact, acceptance of failure, and patience for iteration. Achieving something 10x bigger is counterintuitively easier than 10 percent improvement -- incremental mindsets keep us tethered to existing tools, while 10x goals free us because we know we cannot get there with what we have now. Waymo has completed more than 33 million miles on public roads. Alphabet X's Project Loon used high-altitude balloons for internet connectivity in Kenya and Peru. Lego expanded beyond physical bricks into films, games, and theme parks.

**Strategy as an Infinite Game.** Finite games end with winners and losers. Infinite games go on forever, and the goal is not to win but to keep playing. The finite player asks "How can we maximize this quarter's profit?" The infinite player asks "What investments must we make now to ensure we're still delighting customers a decade from now?"

**Play the Movie Backward.** Moderna CEO Stephane Bancel describes projecting three, five, or ten years into the future, envisioning the destination, then rewinding to map necessary milestones. As Bezos has said: "Friends congratulate me after a quarterly-earnings announcement... And I'll say, 'Thank you, but that quarter was baked three years ago. I'm working on a quarter that'll happen in three years' time right now.'"

**Iterative De-Risking.** Future-focus is "counting cards, not gambling." Lean Startup principles help: building minimum viable products to test assumptions rapidly, conducting rapid experiments, and maintaining willingness to pivot. Projects with slow learning loops are deprioritized in favor of those yielding rapid insights. Success is measured by "learning per dollar."

**Ambidexterity.** Simultaneously pursue incremental and discontinuous innovation. Efficiently exploit existing business to fund long-term explorations. Dream big but start small. Establish processes for data-driven evaluation to guide long-term investments.

**Willingness to Be Misunderstood.** Amazon puts it this way, underscoring commitment to long-term goals that challenge conventional wisdom -- like seemingly cannibalizing the book business with an e-reader. Octopus leaders need to love the 1 percent shot at $1 billion more than the $1 million guaranteed.

## Key Principles

- Disruption is not a future threat -- it is woven into the fabric of today's operations
- 10x thinking frees teams from incremental constraints and inspires ownership, curiosity, and hard work
- Connect ambitious goals with concrete steps needed now to start getting there
- Working backward from the opportunity is preferred over limiting vision to what is currently possible
- The faster you learn, the more likely you are to succeed, no matter how big the goal

## Real-World Examples

- **Intel**: Focused on sustaining innovations while competitors captured the mobile market
- **Alphabet X (Astro Teller)**: Choice A vs. Choice B thought experiment; Project Loon; "counting cards, not gambling"
- **Amazon (Jeff Bezos)**: "I'm working on a quarter that'll happen in three years' time"; "willing to be misunderstood for long periods"; Just Walk Out technology
- **Moderna (Stephane Bancel)**: "Play the movie backward" -- project the future then map milestones in reverse
- **Waymo**: 33 million+ self-driving miles on public roads
- **Lego**: Expansion from physical bricks into films, video games, and theme parks
- **Disney MagicBand**: Innovation beyond perceived boundaries
- **Blockbuster, Toys "R" Us, Sears, Xerox PARC**: All saw or invented the future but chose to focus on the present

## Practical Takeaways

1. Visualize future scenarios vividly: hold sessions imagining industry, customer needs, and technology in three, five, or ten years using pretotypes, storytelling, and visuals
2. Think 10x, not 10 percent: when presented with a proposal, ask "What would it take to make the outcome 10x bigger?" and "If we had no limits, what would this look like?"
3. Decouple thinking big from feasibility -- develop ideas first, figure out feasibility quickly and cheaply after
4. Encourage future-casting communities of practice (Tom Soderstrom at NASA/JPL created a team of futurists solving today's problems with tomorrow's tech)
5. Align incentives and resource allocation: revise executive compensation to include three-to-five-year horizons and innovation outcomes, protect exploration budgets with stage-gated funding
