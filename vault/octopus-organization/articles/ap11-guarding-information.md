## The Antipattern

Le-Brun and Werner describe organizations that build up bureaucratic restrictions around the knowledge of, access to, and use of strategically important data and information. The results are poor decisions based on incomplete knowledge, loss of competitive advantage and missed opportunities, and an emerging culture of distrust and control that undermines outcome ownership.

The old idea that "information is power" translates into a culture where data and insights -- especially those that could shift internal power balances, expose underperformance, or contradict established narratives -- become sequestered. Access is not merely restricted; it is guarded, flowing only through permission or special dispensation, sometimes shaped by gatekeepers acting on their own perception of need.

## Why It Happens

The authors identify five root causes:

1. **McGregor's "Theory X" mindset**: An implicit assumption that employees cannot be trusted with sensitive information, might misinterpret it, or leak it.
2. **Fear of losing control or status**: If knowledge is perceived as power, sharing dilutes personal or departmental influence.
3. **Fear of scrutiny or judgment**: Sharing unfavorable performance data creates vulnerability, especially where "shooting the messenger" is a known risk.
4. **Perfectionism and image management**: A desire to share only polished, "safe" information, hiding messy work-in-progress reality.
5. **Historical precedent and structural inertia**: "We've always done it this way." Silos become fortresses.

Le-Brun and Werner note this is not always deliberate or malicious -- often it is a by-product of deeply ingrained bureaucratic structures. Aaron Dignan (*Brave New Work*) observes this tendency "almost always leads to missed opportunities and sometimes scandal."

The consequences are severe: unvarnished customer feedback marketing holds onto never reaches product teams; early warnings from frontline staff are buried by managers. Many organizations invest heavily in analytics tools but keep most data behind gatekeepers, exposing only "safe" operational data.

Sociologist Ron Westrum's framework on information flow cultures is central:
- **Pathological (power-oriented)**: Information hoarded, messengers shot. Example: Tom Scully threatened to fire the Chief Actuary of the American Medicare Program for sharing disturbing budget estimates.
- **Bureaucratic (rule-oriented)**: Information constrained by cumbersome formal channels. Example: Radiologists reluctant to share early evidence of battered children due to limited influence beyond hospital walls.
- **Generative (mission-oriented)**: Good information flow, high cooperation, trust, new ideas welcomed, failures treated as learning.

## The Octopus Way

Octopus Organizations believe the greatest risk is not from sharing too much but from sharing too little, too slowly. Trust is not a reward for good behavior but a prerequisite for it.

- **Cultivate generative culture**: Foster Ron Westrum's third type -- mission-oriented, with good information flow, high cooperation, and failures treated as learning.
- **Shift from scarcity to abundance mindset**: Move beyond "information is a limited resource to be guarded" to information symmetry where all parties have access to the same relevant information. This goes beyond data dashboards -- it is about changing hearts and minds.
- **The calculated strategy of trust**: Trust begets responsibility and engagement. Transparency fuels agility and alignment. Collective intelligence builds more value than siloed expertise -- "the next brilliant idea or crucial warning could come from anyone, anywhere."
- **Manage the transition deliberately**:
  - Build robust psychological safety so that bad news and imperfect work-in-progress information are met with inquiry, not blame.
  - Establish clear, minimal rules for genuinely confidential information (personal data, legally privileged information, intellectual property) -- narrowly scoped and regularly reviewed.
  - Focus on principles, not just policies. General Stanley McChrystal: "Share information until you're afraid it's illegal."
  - Design for "pull," not just "push" -- well-organized internal wikis, powerful search tools, and clear dashboards so individuals can pull what they need. The emphasis is on access, not forced consumption.
- **Reward information sharing**: Redesign performance metrics to reflect the value of contributing to collective knowledge (contributions to internal knowledge bases, cross-functional project participation), not just individual achievements.

## Key Principles

1. The greatest risk is sharing too little, too slowly -- not sharing too much
2. Trust is a prerequisite for good behavior, not a reward for it
3. Default to "need to share" unless there is a compelling, clearly articulated reason for restriction
4. Leaders' symbolic actions (what they ask for, punish, reward, and share) create the information climate
5. Design for pull (accessible, discoverable) not push (overwhelming, forced)
6. Generative culture (Westrum) produces better outcomes than pathological or bureaucratic cultures

## Real-World Examples

- **American Medicare Program (Tom Scully)**: Bureau Chief threatened to fire the Chief Actuary for sharing disturbing budget estimates -- a stark pathological culture example.
- **Radiologists and battered children**: Bureaucratic culture prevented sharing early evidence of child abuse beyond hospital walls.
- **General Stanley McChrystal**: "Share information until you're afraid it's illegal" -- pushing boundaries of traditional secrecy.
- **Handelsbanken** (Swedish bank): Long exemplified radical transparency with decentralized P&Ls and open information access.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Be a data sharer**: Visibly share information you might have previously kept close -- strategic dilemmas, financial performance warts and all, learnings from failures.
- **Challenge "need to know" culture**: Default to "need to share." Create forums where restriction reasons can be openly challenged.
- **Decriminalize bad news**: When problems are shared, the first reaction must be "Thank you for bringing this to our attention," followed by inquiry and support, not blame.
- **Cocreate "transparency tenets"**: Develop principles like "We share by default" and "We treat information as an organizational asset, not personal property."
- **Focus on strategically critical information**: Do not just democratize "safe" data. Prioritize strategy explanations, progress against key goals, and customer insights. Survey employees about what information they feel they are missing.
- **Simplify access**: Ensure sharing is not technically or bureaucratically cumbersome through user-friendly platforms and intuitive information organization.
- **Encourage storytelling with data**: Train leaders to communicate challenges and learnings through honest, data-backed narratives.
- **Rewrite incentives**: Build information sharing, collaborative data use, and transparent reporting into performance expectations and reward systems.
- **Conduct "information autopsies"**: After failures caused by poor information flow, run blameless postmortems to understand systemic breakdowns.
