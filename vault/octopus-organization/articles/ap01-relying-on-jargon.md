# Antipattern #1: Relying on Jargon

*Clear language creates collective understanding and a shared purpose.*

## The Antipattern

Organizations default to vague, impressive-sounding language -- "corporatese" -- avoiding the rigorous work of creating genuine, shared understanding and having difficult conversations. The results are a confused workforce pulling in many directions, disunity and wasted resources, and leadership seen as evasive, manipulative, or dishonest.

Le-Brun and Werner provide three real examples straight from their interactions with companies:

- "In light of the dynamic and unprecedented economic landscape, we have implemented a multifaceted strategy to right-size our core competencies..."
- "By harnessing the power of integrated and disruptive technologies and embracing an end-to-end paradigm shift in our business model through a game-changing digital transformation..."
- "By leveraging cutting-edge AI-driven solutions and fostering a culture of agile innovation, we're pivoting toward a data-centric ecosystem..."

The absurdity becomes clear if you imagine a leader asking an employee what they are working on today, and the employee replying: "I'm embracing an end-to-end paradigm shift in our business model."

## Why It Happens

The authors identify several root causes:

- **Genuine lack of clarity among leaders** -- they do not actually know what they mean
- **Fear of vulnerable, direct communication** -- an inability to be vulnerable and say the hard thing
- **Intellectual insecurity** -- masking it with impressive-sounding phrases
- **Path of least resistance** -- in cultures where management-speak is the expected dialect
- **A desire to hide or blend in** rather than truly lead or connect

Generic, jargony language is "endlessly interpretable." Terms like "fusion teams," "agile," or "data-enabled" mean ten different things to ten different people. One CEO candidly admitted there was little behind the words "AI transformation," but the perception had added $5 to the stock price -- a bump that will disappear when millions of dollars go away with no meaningful transformation.

Some words betray Tin Man ways of working. "Cascade" and "roll out" reflect top-down processes. "Marching orders" and "delivery team" suggest employees are merely recipients of instructions. Most pernicious is corporatese developed to soften or mask bad news: "rightsizing," "cost avoidance," "unallocated," and the "laughably delusional 'promotions outward.'"

As Professor Scott Galloway calls it: "yogababble."

## The Octopus Way

Octopus Organizations speak in **plain words**. They eschew euphemisms, opting for simplicity. They do not "face headwinds"; they say "The project is off track and we're building a plan to try and get it back on track." This directness stems from a culture that values truth over comfort.

Key Octopus behaviors:

- **Ground initiatives in purpose** -- explain the Why before the How or What. When the underlying intent is clear, jargon naturally falls away.
- **Internalize Einstein's wisdom** -- if you can't explain something simply, you don't understand it well enough
- **Do not hide behind words** -- no presenting negatives as positives through euphemism
- **Use storytelling** -- humans are narrative creatures. Storytelling naturally resists jargon and captures people's imagination. Robert Barrios at E&J Gallo Winery exemplified this with the emotion in his voice and the metaphors he used.

| Tin Man says | Octopus says |
|---|---|
| "...multifaceted strategy to right-size our core competencies, resulting in enhanced operational efficiencies..." | "We're facing challenges. We should have anticipated some of these market shifts sooner, and now we need to make tough decisions, including layoffs." |
| "...game-changing digital transformation...unlock unprecedented value creation opportunities..." | "We're investing in new practices and technologies with the help of external experts. Our aim is to find new ways to serve our customers and improve how we work." |
| "...leveraging cutting-edge AI-driven solutions...data-centric ecosystem..." | "We invested in AI software that, while complex to implement, we believe has strong potential to improve our productivity, enhance customer engagement, and keep us competitive. There will be a learning curve." |

## Key Principles

- **The "yogababble" test** (Scott Galloway): If it sounds like it could apply to any company in any industry, it is jargon, not communication
- **Jargon as a symptom**: Corporatese is often a symptom of deeper issues -- lack of clarity, fear, intellectual insecurity, or a desire to hide
- **Language shapes culture**: Words like "cascade" and "roll out" embed top-down thinking; words like "ownership" and "curiosity" invite agency
- **Facts tell but stories sell**: Storytelling creates emotional connections, buy-in, and action

## Real-World Examples

- **E&J Gallo Winery** -- Robert Barrios demonstrated how storytelling and emotional communication inspired others, even people who did not work at the company
- **Steve Jobs** -- famously avoided jargon, communicating with clarity and passion that forged emotional connections
- **Anonymous CEO** -- candidly admitted "AI transformation" language had added $5 to stock price despite having little substance behind it
- **John Kotter** -- management guru who objects to words like "consult" and "train" because most people know more about their own organizations than outsiders do
- **World Health Organization** -- developed communication protocols as checklists to ensure critical health information is universally understood

## Practical Takeaways

1. **Conduct writing and storytelling workshops.** Find your good writers and have them review documents or run workshops. Find people who tell good stories and have them share their mental process.
2. **Hunt down corporatese and reward its elimination.** Build a living list of jargon. Gamify with "bullshit bingo" cards. Have people select a non-word of the week that triggers charitable donations when used.
3. **Connect every initiative to a concrete customer or business outcome.** Refuse to proceed until impact is articulated in simple, tangible terms. Instead of "leveraging AI for enhanced ecosystem optimization," define: "We will use AI to reduce customer support wait times by 20% by Q4."
4. **Build a "clarity checklist."** Before sending any important communication: "Could this be shorter?" "Would my message be clear to someone outside my team?"
5. **Run "the toddler test" for all key messages.** Explain it to a curious young person. If you cannot convey the essence without jargon, it is not ready.
