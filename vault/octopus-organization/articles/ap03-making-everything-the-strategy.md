# Antipattern #3: Making "Everything" the Strategy

*A sharp focus on just a few durable customer needs creates unique value.*

## The Antipattern

Organizations try to be great at too many things or pursue a strategy with too many priorities and without making necessary trade-offs. The results are fragmented, conflicting direction, resources spread thin, inability to adapt, and no clear vision of success.

Le-Brun and Werner call this the **"Tina Turner" approach to strategy** -- companies that just want to be "simply the best" at everything. Strategies sound like they want to do everything, serve everyone, win everywhere. Phrases like "deliver value to your customers," "achieve sustainable growth," and "be the number one choice for employees" could apply to almost any company in any industry.

**The McDonald's test**: If someone found your strategy document left behind at a table at McDonald's and handed it to a competitor, would it cause major harm to your business? If not, you have strategy work to do.

## Why It Happens

- **Strategy by committee**: Ideas and opinions are poured into a strategic melting pot and boiled down to broad, inclusive statements to ensure everyone feels their voice was heard and gets a piece of the operational action. Strategies become a conglomeration of compromises.
- **Disconnect from execution**: Two-day offsites followed by three months of leadership review disconnects strategy from execution, creating a sense that strategy is something you set once and then turn to execution.
- **The "house-shaped diagram" syndrome**: Customer centricity, operational excellence, financial prudence, sustainability, and people all placed on a PowerPoint with pillars and foundations. It looks impressive but is "the same high-level nothing."
- **Comfortable frameworks**: The sheer availability of strategy frameworks means you can find a methodology that feels safe and comfortable, but a comfortable strategy delivers small results.
- **Contradictory advice**: No shortage of contradictory guidance -- strategies should be for one year, or three, or ten to fifteen; every business unit needs their own or you need one grand strategy. Gary Hamel: "The dirty little secret of the strategy industry is that it doesn't have any theory of strategy creation."
- **Only 28% of executives and middle managers** with execution responsibilities could name three strategic priorities (MIT Sloan). At the edge of the organization, a mere 13% of supervisors could.

The classic cautionary tale: railroad companies defined themselves as "being in the road industry" -- a safe, generic strategy that left them unable to respond when air transport emerged.

## The Octopus Way

**"Strategy equals choice."** -- Benedetto Vigna, CEO of Ferrari. Implicit in that choice is the choice of where *not* to play.

Octopus Organizations:

1. **Deliberately choose where to play and how they will win.** "The greatest business failures often come not from playing the game poorly, but from continuing to excel at things that no longer matter."

2. **Vigorously and continuously debate conscious trade-offs.** They show courage in pruning markets, customers, or strategic initiatives. Good trade-offs lead to better results as fewer resources are wasted.

3. **Focus on durable customer needs over current trends.** Seth Godin calls strategy a philosophy of asking "Who will we become?" and "Who will we be of service to?" Amazon's long-term strategic thinking is based on the idea that customers will never want to pay more, have less selection, or wait longer. Jeff Bezos: "If everything you do needs to work on a three-year time horizon, then you're competing against a lot of people. But if you're willing to invest on a seven-year time horizon, you're now competing against a fraction of those people."

4. **Recognize strategic inflection points.** Rita McGrath in *Seeing Around Corners* highlights cultivating "peripheral vision" and actively looking for weak signals on the edges of the current market.

5. **Support activities that deliver unique value** -- staying away from generic, safe, imitative strategies.

6. **Use clear and specific language to communicate strategy**, not PowerPoint "houses." Jargon invites cynicism; general statements are interpretable.

7. **Leaders take charge of strategy communication.** Benedikt Bohm at Dynafit says 80% of his job is to create strategic clarity: "The amount you invest is the speed you save when it comes through in the action." Rather than hand off to comms teams, he works with his leadership team to distill key messages and a storyline.

8. **Leaders listen.** Ferrari's Benedetto Vigna connected with 300+ individuals across the organization, signed 700+ NDAs to look outside, visited China, Korea, and Japan, used "brain writing" instead of brainstorming ("taking into account introverts"), was fast, and communicated strategy at all levels himself.

## Key Principles

- **"Strategy equals choice"** (Benedetto Vigna, Ferrari): Strategy without trade-offs is not strategy
- **The McDonald's test**: If your strategy document could belong to a competitor, it is not differentiated
- **Durable needs over trends** (Bezos): Focus on what customers will always want
- **Strategic inflection points** (Rita McGrath): Cultivate peripheral vision for weak signals
- **Strategy as a compass, not a map** (Seth Godin): A plan choosing what to do today to make tomorrow better
- **Brain writing over brainstorming** (Benedetto Vigna): Written input accounts for introverts and produces better strategy input

## Real-World Examples

- **Ferrari** -- CEO Benedetto Vigna connected with 300+ people, signed 700+ NDAs, visited Asia, used brain writing, communicated strategy personally at all levels
- **Dynafit** -- CEO Benedikt Bohm says 80% of his CEO role is creating strategic clarity; draws parallels to extreme mountaineering where obsessive weight reduction creates clarity and speed
- **Amazon** -- Strategy anchored to durable customer needs (lower prices, wider selection, faster delivery); Bezos on the advantage of a seven-year time horizon
- **Netflix** -- Pivot to original content required trading off billions in short-term profit; cracking down on password sharing was a trade-off between subscriber growth velocity and revenue maximization
- **Dollar Shave Club** -- Disrupted the razor market with a D2C subscription model, totally unique in consumer-packaged goods, leading to Unilever acquisition
- **Johnson & Johnson / Janssen** -- Bill Height oversaw $10B R&D budget; in 2012 shifted from treating diseases to the durable need of preventing them, informed by convergence of life sciences and biosensors
- **Railroad companies** -- Classic case of generic strategy ("being in the road industry") leading to inability to respond to air transport

## Practical Takeaways

1. **Anchor on durable problems.** Write down with your leadership team what you believe the durable needs of your customers will be over the next ten years. Compare perspectives and debate differences to develop a shared set. Prioritize where you play and how you win against these needs.
2. **Make your strategy accessible.** Write a two-page narrative describing strategy in simple language. Test with employees. Use feedback to iterate on clarity of thought, content, and language.
3. **Communicate continuously.** Goal: every employee knows your top three strategic priorities. Hold monthly sessions where teams share their interpretation of the strategy and challenge it. Use a "golden thread exercise" -- ask employees to draw a line from their daily work to strategic priorities. Notice where they struggle.
4. **Write "this over that" statements.** Each statement explains how your strategy values one thing over another. If these trade-offs do not cause strong debates and create defensible opposites, you do not have a strategy.
5. **Test with a "competitor swap" exercise.** Remove your logo from your strategy document, replace with a competitor's. If it still makes sense, it is not differentiated. Iterate until it sounds absurd coming from a competitor.
