## The Antipattern

Tin Man organizations prioritize "brilliant jerk" star performers at the cost of cross-functional collaboration. The results are internal competition and excessive politicking, people focused on themselves rather than customer impact, a lack of teamwork, and hindered knowledge sharing.

## Why It Happens

HR focuses on amassing high performers through "talent" recruitment, over-indexing on skills and expertise over social and emotional capabilities. The assumption is simple math: more super chickens means more eggs. But evolutionary biologist William Muir's landmark study proved otherwise -- when he split chickens into average performers and "super chickens" (most productive egg layers), after six generations the average flock thrived while all but three super chickens had pecked one another to death.

Tin Man organizations also create the perfect habitat for brilliant jerks through **functional silos**. When you organize around specialized departments -- sales chasing growth targets, operations cutting costs, marketing protecting turf -- you build separate chicken coops. Functional identity ("I am a marketing person") weighs stronger than organizational identity ("I am a customer champion"). Each silo rewards its own star performers who never have to prove they can actually work with others.

Even when Tin Men attempt to bridge silos with teams, they fundamentally misunderstand what makes teams work. They treat teams as temporary task forces -- collections of their best individual performers handed tasks to complete, not problem-solvers that collectively own challenges. Matrix teams create split loyalties, divided focus, and collaboration challenges. Harvard research found that avoiding hiring a single toxic "super chicken" saves a company more than $12,400 in turnover costs -- more than double the gain from hiring a top 1 percent performer.

## The Octopus Way

Octopus Organizations recognize that the fundamental unit of value creation is the team, and they redefine what makes someone talented in a team setting.

**The Shane Battier Effect.** Professional basketball player Shane Battier had mediocre individual stats, yet every team he joined dramatically improved when he played. As Michael Lewis noted, "when he is on the court, his teammates get better, often a lot better, and his opponents get worse." This "teammateship" through subtle contributions is the very opposite of super chicken.

**Humble, Hungry, Smart.** Octopus Organizations hire individuals who score high on three essential characteristics: They are humble (manage ego, share credit, define success collectively), they are hungry (want to do more, learn more, own more), and they are smart (ask good questions, actively listen, show good judgment in group dynamics). These qualities replace the "blast radius" of a brilliant jerk with the "halo radius" of a high-performing team member.

**Long-Lived Cross-Functional Teams.** Octopus Organizations shift from functional silos to long-lived cross-functional teams that operate like mini-companies, each taking ownership of a specific customer problem, product, or value stream. The team, not the silo, provides an individual's home and sense of identity. These teams serve as learning amplifiers, early signal detectors, and pattern-breaking forces.

**DBS Bank's Journey.** Singapore bank DBS exemplifies the path to long-lived teams: first, leaders changed language from vertical silos to horizontal collaboration; then shifted to "two-in-a-box" platforms with joint business and technology ownership (thirty-three platforms by 2018); and finally introduced "Managing through Journeys" (MtJ), focusing on end-to-end customer outcomes with cross-functional squads led by "mini-CEOs." By 2024, approximately four hundred leaders shared common dashboards for mutual accountability. The Treasury Markets journey brought functions together to transform foreign exchange, resulting in 15 percent revenue growth and high customer satisfaction scores.

## Key Principles

- The team is the fundamental unit of value creation, not the individual
- Google's Project Aristotle found psychological safety is the single most important factor in team effectiveness
- Long-lived teams accumulate knowledge, increase innovation, work faster without silos, and nurture long-term ownership
- As Allan Kelly notes in Team Topologies, breaking up high-performing teams is "worse than vandalism: it is corporate psychopathy"
- Align training, performance assessments, incentives, and rewards to teams over individuals (Nucor pays bonuses to teams, not individuals)

## Real-World Examples

- **William Muir's Super Chicken Study**: Average flock thrived while super chicken flock self-destructed through competition
- **Shane Battier (NBA)**: Mediocre individual stats but dramatically elevated every team he joined
- **DBS Bank (Singapore)**: Evolved from silos to "two-in-a-box" platforms to cross-functional squads with "mini-CEOs"
- **Google's Project Aristotle**: Identified psychological safety as the top factor in team effectiveness
- **Google AdWords Team**: Completely reimagined the advertising model by breaking free of individual cognitive biases
- **Navy SEALs**: After-action reviews where open discussion of failures leads to exponential learning
- **Richard Branson**: Disguised himself as a taxi driver to assess how senior leadership candidates treated people
- **Datadog and Atlassian**: Screen for humility and team orientation in hiring

## Practical Takeaways

1. Frankly ask: "Who are the brilliant jerks?" and "Do people get ahead here by doing great work and helping others, or by undermining colleagues?"
2. Make it clear during hiring that jerks are not welcome -- add interview questions assessing how candidates treat people and handle conflict
3. Implement team formation protocols including "letters of understanding" where members document working preferences and strengths
4. Create team performance measures including psychological safety scores, collaboration levels, and knowledge-sharing effectiveness
5. Run team strengths spotlights in meetings -- rotate through members sharing a strength they bring to the team
6. Develop leadership training focused on guiding teams through storming-forming-norming-performing stages
