## The Antipattern

Tin Man organizations give responsibility to people without providing the conditions for them to execute effectively and safely. The results are cynicism and disengagement, a feeling of powerlessness that reduces the inclination to take initiative, slowed decision-making, reduced agility and innovation, and resources wasted on oversight rather than value delivery.

## Why It Happens

Ownership has two sides: someone needs to give it, and someone else needs to take it. Much can go wrong between these two sides.

**Failure to give ownership manifests as:** demanding frequent detailed updates, asking for input but stopping short of transferring decision-making authority, inadvertently withholding critical information or resources, defining how work should be done or redoing work, overruling decisions others should be making, and maintaining oversight powers that prevent meaningful decisions.

**Failure to take ownership manifests as:** deferring decisions to leadership, building excessive consensus, incorporating all input rather than making trade-offs, avoiding decisions, deflecting responsibility, and thinking "what would leadership want?"

The authors observe this regularly. One COO and sponsor of a major technology platform told them his product teams were empowered, then was seen screaming at them and threatening to fire individuals. Another leader got angry at a new owner for making a bad decision, only to realize she had failed to enable that person by not providing enough strategic context. A third COO shrugged his shoulders: "The CEO told me to do it. You might as well get entry-level staff into our roles if you're going to get told what to do."

Leaders struggle to relinquish ownership due to perfectionism, habit of directing rather than coaching, fear that team failures will reflect poorly on them, envy of a team's success, loss of importance if teams function independently, and lack of coaching skills. Those shunning ownership do so due to habit, lack of clarity on boundaries, risk/reward imbalance, resource limitations, and organizational complexity.

As Shonda Rhimes puts it: "If you hired people to do a job, you should let them do that job. When people are sitting around waiting for you to tell them what to do, it's painful for them and for you."

## The Octopus Way

**Acknowledge Artificial Ownership Exists.** Octopus Organizations examine behaviors for what is driving them. For instance: "Progress reports seem to be used to relieve anxiety around relinquishing control." Or: "We didn't have a good definition of ownership, which led to the assumption that input alone is ownership."

**Define What Ownership Actually Means.** True ownership is not about some vague feeling of being "heard." It is the authority to make decisions and solve problems toward a clear purpose and outcome, with clear roles, responsibilities, and boundaries, and the confidence that acting in this context will not be career limiting. This includes making information accessible, increasing freedom over resources, giving latitude on how to operate and solve problems, and being clear about what decision-making is in scope.

**Use Principles and Guardrails, Not Procedures.** Instead of detailed procedures, establish principles and guardrails -- customer-first values, financial boundaries, quality standards -- that serve as a compass rather than a map. This allows owners to make contextual decisions while staying aligned with organizational objectives.

**Switch from Command to Coach.** Leaders become enablers who understand through dialogue where external dependencies or lack of resources are barriers, then address them. They provide the "commander's intent" -- a broad definition of what a successful mission looks like in its end state -- and let teams determine how to achieve it. Former McDonald's senior director Sue Pittacora highlighted how leaders consciously checked their desire to take control and give answers, instead positioning themselves as barrier removers.

**Serve as Clarifiers.** As London Stock Exchange CEO Dame Julia Hoggett puts it, being clear about "the exam question to be answered" is critical. Acknowledging your own distance from the details and the team's closeness to them puts them in a better position to make decisions.

**Recognize the Fragility of Ownership.** As Johannesburg Stock Exchange Clear CEO Alicia Greenwood notes, "It takes a lot to build a culture of ownership but very little to destroy it." The minute employees see command and control that contradicts what they have been promised, there will be problems.

## Key Principles

- Either you trust your people, or you do not -- if you do, get out of their way
- Ownership means building a system where trust is the default, not the exception
- Your influence cannot scale through prescriptive direction -- it scales through enabling others
- Can they get it 80 percent right and be good enough? Will the minor "damage" be above the waterline of the ship?
- Artificial ownership is worse than not giving hope of sharing ownership in the first place
- Reed Hastings: "I pride myself on making fewer and fewer decisions. Sometimes I can go a whole quarter without making a decision."
- Sam Walton: "When employees think, act, and feel like owners, they make better decisions -- decisions that are in the long-term interest of the company."

## Real-World Examples

- **McDonald's (Sue Pittacora)**: Leaders consciously checked their desire to take control, positioning themselves as barrier removers
- **Netflix (Reed Hastings)**: "Sometimes I can go a whole quarter without making a decision"
- **London Stock Exchange (Dame Julia Hoggett)**: Being clear about "the exam question" as a clarifier for teams
- **Sibanye-Stillwater (Werner Swanepoel)**: Driving ownership at the edge by identifying activated people, making them pioneers, putting them on a pedestal to inspire others
- **Patagonia**: Commander's intent -- "Build the best product, cause no unnecessary harm, and use business to inspire and implement solutions to the environmental crisis" -- not prescriptive but meaningful to every department
- **Michael Bungay Stanier's Coaching Questions**: "What outcomes are you hoping for?" "What options have you already thought about?" "What support do you need?" "And what else?"

## Practical Takeaways

1. See how long you can go without making a decision -- resist command-and-control urges
2. Use the power of language: frame requests as open-ended questions, acknowledge initiative, discuss outcomes not methods, reinforce your role as enabler
3. Frame the intent: document and communicate what success looks like and why it matters, then let teams determine how
4. Invest in coaching: respond with questions rather than solutions when teams ask for help
5. Reward smart risk-taking: publicly call out learning from failure and decision-making
6. Conduct ownership reviews: assess decision-making patterns, identify opportunities to push authority closer to the customer, mitigate unnecessary dependencies
7. Track ownership metrics: if more than 20 percent of decisions are reviewed by higher management per quarter, ownership is weak
