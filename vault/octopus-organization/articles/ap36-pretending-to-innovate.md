# Antipattern #36: Pretending to Innovate

## The Antipattern

Tin Man organizations treat innovation as a bolt-on, separate, and structured activity -- in part driven by a desire to be seen as "innovative." A 2024 study found that a record 83 percent of executives rank innovation as a top-three priority, yet only 3 percent of companies are "innovation ready" to translate this priority into results -- a dramatic drop from the already-low 20 percent in 2022.

Le-Brun and Werner identify three forms of separation:

- **Physical separation**: Innovation labs become islands distant from actual business. 90 percent of them fail, including those at Walmart, Ford, IKEA, and Disney. Born from a mix of FOMO and helplessness, they close when they fail to demonstrate returns. The next go-to solutions -- design-thinking offsites and hackathons for the chosen few -- become "fun days that do little more than create neat ideas divorced from operational reality."
- **Structural separation**: Innovation becomes the job of "special people" while everyone else does "real work." This implicitly tells the vast majority that innovation isn't their responsibility. As Virgin Experience Days CEO Christoph Homann says, "It's like buying all the gym kit for home but doing nothing with it."
- **Process separation**: Innovation gets treated as a structured, predictable process. But as Alphabet X CEO Astro Teller told the authors: "You can't get there by starting with efficiency and trying to insert magic into the process. It's literally impossible. You have to start on the magic side... and then insert rigor very carefully so as to not kill the magic."

The result is **innovation theater** -- "the equivalent of cosplay for corporates," like the Wizard of Oz pulling levers behind the curtain. The most significant cost is the thousands of small, brilliant ideas from people connecting with real problems that are never born because those people have been told innovation isn't their job.

**Tin Man results:**
- Flashy ideas fail to meet real customer needs and don't survive contact with the real world
- Stifled curiosity, untapped innovation potential, and disengagement
- Innovation becomes theater rather than customer value

## Why It Happens

This behavior responds to what Clayton Christensen famously termed "the innovator's dilemma" -- well-managed companies become so adept at serving profitable customers with sustaining innovations that their processes and values prevent them from pursuing disruptive ideas. The separate lab or hackathon becomes a well-intentioned but futile attempt to create space for disruption that the core business is hardwired to reject.

Innovation theater stems from a fundamental misunderstanding equating innovation only with massive, disruptive breakthroughs. Generating novel ideas feels more glamorous than the hard, messy work of understanding customers, defining problems, executing, and integrating.

## The Octopus Way

Octopus Organizations make innovation everyone's job -- integration, not separation. In a 2017 SEC filing, Amazon stated it could not separately disclose R&D spending because its teams are "constantly working to build new... and simultaneously maintain current" products and services. Innovation is happening everywhere, all the time.

**The Amazon Prime story** illustrates five key Octopus behaviors:

1. **Enable the edges.** The spark for Prime came from one engineer, Charlie Ward, frustrated with clunky free shipping options. He had the agency to surface a radical idea from the front lines, close to the customer reality.
2. **Connect small ideas to durable needs.** Bezos recognized Ward's proposal connected to a "long-lived customer need" -- customers will always want things faster and cheaper. He championed it as project "Futurama."
3. **Practice ambidexterity.** Even during critical pre-Christmas stability problems, Bezos sheltered the Futurama team, allowing exploration without being crushed by operational urgencies.
4. **Innovate the organization itself.** Prime created tensions with existing incentive models for category managers measured on quarterly profit. Amazon changed the metrics to align with the new model -- true innovation often requires changing how an organization operates, not just the product.
5. **Play the infinite game.** Prime became a platform for more innovation: third-party merchants, Prime Video, Music, and Prime Day -- treating initial success as a foundation to build upon, not a one-and-done launch.

**Key Octopus behaviors at any scale:**
- Cultivate builders with local ownership and cross-functional teams
- Relentlessly work backward from real customer problems
- Lead with context, not control -- set vision and strategy, don't dictate solutions
- Provide tools to everyone: frameworks, data, and seed funding to remove barriers
- Foster creative abrasion -- make it safe to challenge ideas and learn from mistakes
- Show a bias toward action -- build quickly over perfect presentations

As Thoughtworks CEO Mike Sutcliff notes, it's often 10-15 percent of an organization who drive innovation. The leader's role is to enable that group and the entire organization to contribute through "small-i" innovation.

## Key Principles

1. **Innovation is integration, not separation** -- it cannot be cordoned off in labs, events, or special teams
2. **Small-i innovation matters most** -- process improvements and incremental solutions from any seat are where most value lives
3. **Start with magic, then insert rigor** -- not the other way around (Astro Teller)
4. **Validated learning over raw ideas** -- "What did we learn?" is more important than "What did we build?"
5. **Innovation theater is worse than no innovation** -- it signals innovativeness while killing the real thing

## Real-World Examples

- **Amazon Prime**: From one engineer's frustration to a world-changing platform, demonstrating integrated innovation at every step
- **Amazon SEC filing (2017)**: Could not separately disclose R&D spending because innovation is embedded in everything
- **Failed innovation labs**: Walmart, Ford, IKEA, Disney -- 90% failure rate
- **Virgin Experience Days**: CEO Homann's "gym kit" analogy for unused innovation capability
- **Alphabet X**: Astro Teller on starting with magic, not efficiency
- **Clayton Christensen**: The innovator's dilemma framework explaining why core businesses reject disruption

## Practical Takeaways

1. **Enable everyday problem-solving** -- dedicate 15 minutes in one-on-ones to ask: "What's something you're curious about that might help our customers? What's getting in the way of you trying small things?"
2. **Connect innovation to the edges** -- create a method for anyone to propose an innovation of any size; provide basic templates for hypothesis, measurement, and time frame; reduce approval layers for small bets
3. **Shift focus from ideas to validation** -- stop celebrating raw idea generation; reward validated learning; make "What did we learn?" the key question
4. **Fund the seeds** -- allocate flexible micro-budgets directly to teams for testing ideas, bypassing complex approvals for initial exploration
5. **Change the language** -- move away from "innovation" as a special thing; talk about "improvement," "problem-solving," "experimentation," and "making things better"; Amazon uses "Invent and simplify"
6. **Make problem-solving visible** -- create a low-friction space (Slack channel, wiki page) for teams to post messy, in-progress work: customer problems explored, ugly prototypes, surprising insights
