## The Antipattern

Le-Brun and Werner describe rigid, time-consuming annual budgeting that creates detailed projections about the future that are "virtual guesses." The results: focus shifts from delivering value to winning funds, budgets become inflated through gaming, and organizations suffer inertia tied to fixed budgets allocated months earlier. As serial entrepreneur Peter Hinssen comments on the resulting budgets: "More lies have been told in Excel than anywhere else."

## Why It Happens

The annual budget ritual consumes months and creates an illusion of control. Board reporting cycles demand annual commitments, but whatever control is felt bears little relationship to dynamic market realities. One organization the authors know spent an estimated twenty-five thousand person-hours on its annual budget, only to see 60% of its strategic assumptions invalidated by market shifts within six months.

The authors identify six cognitive biases entrenching budgeting dysfunction:
- **Status quo bias**: "We've always done it this way, and it sort of works."
- **Confirmation bias**: "We always meet our year-end number" (without viewing value delivered).
- **Sunk cost fallacy**: "If we don't continue to budget for this initiative, it will have been a waste."
- **Availability bias**: "The last two companies I worked for used the same process."
- **Loss aversion**: "A new process will create risks and raise unpredictable issues."
- **Anchoring bias**: "I needed this money last year, so it's probably reasonable to assume the same this year."

These biases are not personal failings but a function of misaligned incentives, creating a principal-agent problem where managers prioritize personal security (hitting budget numbers) over the organization's strategic goals.

Five destructive patterns emerge:

1. **Budgets allocated to departments, not value streams**: Most value comes from cross-functional work, yet budgets go to functions, reinforcing artificial scarcity and internal competition. No single leader owns the outcomes.
2. **Annual cycles kill learning and adaptation**: Rigid timelines rarely align with value delivery. Teams delay projects to avoid exceeding budgets or rush year-end "use it or lose it" spending.
3. **Process perfection overtakes outcome focus**: Organizations obsess over CapEx vs. OpEx classifications, arbitrary spending rules (contractor rate caps), and year-end hiring freezes that disrupt critical projects.
4. **Politics crowd out performance**: Political skills outweigh analytical rigor. Status becomes conflated with budget size rather than value delivered. Sandbagging and slush funds ("marketing innovation alignment initiative") become rational survival tactics.
5. **Budget opacity breeds mistrust**: Organizations guard budgets as confidential, preventing insights that could improve allocation and identify collaboration opportunities.

## The Octopus Way

- **Move from static budgets to dynamic forecasting**: Former Capital One CFO Chris Hennesey notes static budgets are "dead on arrival." Pilot rolling forecasts (quarterly, looking five to eight quarters out) focusing on three to five key business drivers. Use scenario planning (best/worst/likely). Companies like Equinor and Maersk use dynamic forecasting as an anticipation tool, not a control mechanism, separating forecasts from fixed targets.
- **Establish cross-functional funding governance**: Regular gatherings where funding requests are debated on strategic merit, learnings, and potential ROI. Growth boards, portfolio review boards, or value councils bring focus to debate, requests, and changes.
- **Fund outcomes, not outputs**: Shift from "launch three new features" (output) to "increase active new feature usage by new customers by 15% within thirty days" (outcome). Define outcomes collaboratively with accountable teams.
- **Invest in capacity through stable teams**: "Capacity funding" provides predictable costs but immense flexibility in work undertaken. Identify a critical value stream (like "new customer onboarding"), form a cross-functional team with a clear mission and outcome-based targets. Their budget is their operational cost.
- **Use venture capital-style stage-gate funding for innovation**: Teams unlock contingent budget tranches by proving hypotheses with data. This mirrors how VCs fund startups. Foster psychological safety so teams can report learning from "failed" hypotheses without penalty. Parts of Bosch and 3M have adopted such models.
- **Combat cognitive biases systematically**: Use external perspectives, devil's advocate processes, and pre-mortems. Define explicit guardrails for investments over certain thresholds ("must deliver measurable customer value within six months"). Finance teams should continually ask: "What positive outcome does this rule enable?" If the answer is merely administrative, challenge the rule.
- **Counter gaming through radical transparency**: Publish funding rationales, expected outcomes, and high-level financial performance data. Handelsbanken (Swedish bank) has long exemplified this with decentralized P&Ls and open information access. When everyone knows what funding is spent on and why, teams identify collaboration opportunities and avoid duplication.

## Key Principles

1. Static annual budgets are "dead on arrival" in a dynamic market
2. Fund outcomes and value streams, not departments and outputs
3. Cognitive biases entrench budgeting dysfunction -- combat them with structured decision frameworks
4. Capacity funding through stable teams provides both predictability and flexibility
5. Radical transparency in funding rationales counters political maneuvering
6. Finance should be a strategic partner in forecasting and scenario planning, not just a guardian of accounting rules

## Practical Takeaways

- **Pilot rolling forecasts**: Start with quarterly forecasts looking five to eight quarters out. Focus on three to five key business drivers rather than line-item detail.
- **Create a funding review board**: Establish regular cross-functional gatherings where funding is debated on strategic merit and outcomes.
- **Shift to outcome-based funding**: For at least one initiative, redefine success metrics from outputs to customer-facing outcomes.
- **Experiment with capacity funding**: For one value stream, fund a stable cross-functional team with outcome-based targets rather than project-based budgets.
- **Run a "bias audit"**: Retrospect on past budget cycles -- how many projects were funded due to prior spend (sunk cost)? Were ambitious ideas scaled back due to low initial allocations (anchoring)? Was there a rush to agreement (bandwagon)?
- **Publish funding rationales**: For significant investments, publish a summary of the rationale and expected impact to counter gaming and surface collaboration opportunities.
- **Challenge arbitrary financial rules**: For each budgeting rule, ask "What positive outcome does this rule enable?" If the answer is merely administrative, challenge it.
