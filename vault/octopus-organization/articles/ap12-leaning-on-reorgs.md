## The Antipattern

Le-Brun and Werner describe organizations that resort to structural change as a plug-and-play tool for change, attempting to improve productivity, shift culture, or mask cost-cutting and layoffs. The results are destruction of valuable knowledge and networks, prolonged productivity drops, attention diverted from customer value to surviving the change, and change fatigue with anxiety-driven behavior.

The authors open with a sardonic composite script of what leaders communicate during reorgs -- and what is left unsaid -- capturing the gap between leadership rhetoric and employee reality.

## Why It Happens

Le-Brun and Werner identify several drivers:

- **Visible decisive leadership**: New leaders see reorgs as a big, visible action. "I'm doing a big thing."
- **Brute-force culture change**: Belief that switching to a matrix model will fix bureaucracy and cultural issues.
- **Smokescreen for cost-cutting**: "What we're really doing is creating synergies and streamlining operations."
- **Imitation**: Companies see industry darlings and copy structures. The "Spotify model" gets photocopied -- roles, structures, and rituals -- while ignoring underlying behavior and mental model changes.

Philip Rosenzweig (*The Halo Effect*) explains why copying fails: success stories oversimplify reality, attributing performance to things that fit a tidy narrative while ignoring behavioral and cultural factors. Zappos succeeded with Holacracy not because the structure was inherently better but because of CEO Tony Hsieh's relentless focus on culture first. When Medium copied the structure without the culture, chaos ensued and the reorg was abandoned.

The fundamental flaw rests on the **Target Operating Model (TOM)** -- a Tin Man concept suggesting a blueprint from "as is" to "to be." These blueprints treat organizations as rational entities where information flows freely and resources naturally align with goals. Reality is messier: with Jonathan Allen (former CTO, Capital One), the authors identified more than 160 changes needed merely to set up cross-functional teams at scale -- changes to process, structure, budget ownership, decision-making, career paths, performance evaluation, resource allocation, tools, on-call rotations, data ownership, meeting rituals, social networks, team development, and hiring.

As Cyert and March note in *Behavioral Theory of Firms*, a firm is a coalition without a singular, static goal -- a living system that must continuously adapt. Reshuffling severs vital informal networks and decimates undocumented collective know-how. Three-quarters of reorgs are considered total failures, leading to regime changes that restart the cycle.

## The Octopus Way

- **Resist bureaucracy and bloat that necessitate reorgs**: Dynafit CEO Benedikt Bohm advocates staying organizationally lean always, not just during crisis. His "gunshot scenario" -- "If you had a gun to your head, would you still keep this department?" -- became a creative exercise fostering entrepreneurial thinking. One team member suggested their own departure; others offered to skip bonuses to avoid reorgs for other teams.
- **Prioritize structural stability with internal flexibility**: Move people and skills naturally where needed. Form and dissolve teams around specific customer needs. Preserve high-performing teams and redirect them rather than disband. As Heidari-Robinson and Heywood say in *ReOrg*: "Change the right bits -- the bits that don't work well -- and leave the rest alone."
- **Nurture informal networks**: Charlotte in finance calls Robbie in IT to unblock issues; Jack in supplier management calls Ida in manufacturing because she knows which suppliers perform. Create opportunities for connections across silos through communities of practice, internal skills marketplaces, and cross-functional forums.
- **Favor small-scale continuous change**: Marty Cagan (Silicon Valley Product Group): "It is easy to do an experiment, but hard to do a transformation project." Identify a good product team leader, demonstrate business results in a quarter or two, and be strategic about the pilot team. "The important point in the transformation is the first win." Amazon's "two-pizza" team structure evolved over two decades.
- **When reorgs are necessary, communicate honestly**: Employees will see through attempts to hide intentions. The authors share Jana's experience at a bank where a locked door appeared in a busy hallway, behind which consultants redesigned the bank. "Staff nervously eyed that door." Promises of improvement after firings were met with educated skepticism that made the change even harder.
- **Communicate early, even without precise timelines**: Leaders think they are doing employees a favor by keeping quiet, but "information flows through the organization in ways they don't even understand." Lay out the planned process first, then communicate benefits once safety is clear.
- **Engage people in the process**: Seek genuine input, clearly explain implications. This is slower than bringing in consultants to present a massive plan, but it leads back to productivity much quicker than being one of the 75% that fail.
- **Focus on intent, not imitation**: Steal ideas but make them uniquely yours. Rabobank HR leader Kiki van den Berg invites leaders to visit organizations with desired cultures, exposing them to the complexity ahead -- perhaps even dissuading them from a reorg.

## Key Principles

1. Structure follows behavior and culture, not the other way around
2. Three-quarters of reorgs fail -- the default should be against reorganizing
3. Informal networks deliver enormous value and are destroyed by reorgs
4. Small, iterative changes outperform big-bang transformations
5. Copying another organization's structure without its culture and behaviors produces chaos
6. Honest, early communication reduces resistance more than polished late announcements

## Real-World Examples

- **Zappos vs. Medium**: Zappos succeeded with Holacracy because of culture-first approach under Tony Hsieh. Medium copied the structure without the culture and abandoned it.
- **Capital One (Jonathan Allen)**: Identified 160+ changes needed merely to set up cross-functional teams at scale.
- **Dynafit (Benedikt Bohm)**: "Gunshot scenario" for challenging unnecessary departments; team members voluntarily proposed their own departure or bonus sacrifice.
- **Jana's bank**: Locked door with consultants behind it breeding anxiety; firings followed by promises met with educated skepticism.
- **Amazon**: Two-pizza team structure evolved over two decades of iterative change, not a single reorg.
- **Rabobank (Kiki van den Berg)**: Leaders invited to visit organizations with desired cultures to understand complexity before committing to reorgs.
- **Marty Cagan**: "The important point in the transformation is the first win."

## Practical Takeaways

- **Define outcomes before reorganizing**: Articulate precise desired outcomes and brainstorm nonstructural alternatives before redrawing the org chart. Have leaders visit organizations that made similar changes successfully.
- **Communicate early and honestly ("trust not terror")**: Share what is and is not known, why a plan is being considered, anticipated timelines, and potential impact -- before having a full plan.
- **Be inclusive and challenging**: Engage the entire leadership team. Have supporters argue against the reorg and vice versa to uncover challenges and build appreciation for the other side.
- **Treat implementation as product management**: Use iterative execution, test assumptions with small experiments, adapt based on feedback and results.
- **Monitor psychological safety continuously**: Regularly assess team cohesion before, during, and after changes using anonymous surveys.
- **Foster communities of practice**: Encourage skills-based groups that share knowledge and build relationships outside formal reporting lines to preserve tribal knowledge.
