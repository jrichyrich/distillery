## The Antipattern

Tin Man organizations try to drive behavior primarily through incentives focused on financial rewards. The results are reduced ownership, collaboration, and curiosity; important un-incentivized work gets neglected; and development is stifled.

## Why It Happens

In Tin Man organizations, "rewards" equal "incentives" -- "The reward I offer you is the incentive to do the work." But incentives and rewards are fundamentally different. **Incentives** are forward-looking propositions ("If you do X, then you get Y") designed to steer behavior toward predefined goals. **Rewards** are retrospective ("the trophy"), bestowed after desired behaviors have occurred, designed to reinforce positive actions and build loyalty.

Trouble brews when organizations conflate the two. Incentive structures echo Douglas McGregor's Theory X -- the assumption that individuals are primarily cogs nudged into action only by external inducements. Such approaches can feel coercive ("If you don't do X, you won't get Y"), spark destructive competition, narrow focus unduly (deprioritizing crucial un-incentivized activities like broader ownership and deep learning), hijack true ownership when metric-chasing overshadows outcomes, promote short-termism over sustainable practices, and trigger unethical behavior.

Frederick Herzberg's two-factor theory provides critical insight: money is primarily a "hygiene factor." Its absence breeds demotivation, but its presence does not automatically ignite motivation. Once people feel fairly compensated, more money yields diminishing returns on drive and satisfaction.

## The Octopus Way

Octopus Organizations harness purpose and intrinsic motivation by providing meaningful work, supportive leadership, and genuine ownership. This taps into McGregor's Theory Y and Daniel Pink's metaphor of solar energy in *Drive*: intrinsic motivation is sustainable, nurturing, and naturally renewing.

**Purpose-Driven Performance.** Luc Hennekens (former CIO at Qantas and Airbus) saw engineers motivated by effusive customer feedback, which fostered pride and allowed them to showcase their knowledge. Former UK government CDO Craig Suckling describes teams finding deep motivation by creating data systems that help vulnerable citizens. Purpose and ownership drive performance more effectively than financial incentives alone.

**Rewards over Incentives.** Octopus Organizations actively inject rewards into the organization, sometimes swapping out incentives entirely. Examples include:
- Zappos's "WoW Awards" for extraordinary customer service
- Southwest Airlines' "Heroes of the Heart" for behind-the-scenes customer experience excellence
- McDonald's Circle of Excellence for cross-functional teams delivering businesswide impacts
- Nike VP Michael Newton used company-wide recognition for creating new customer value, more impactful than monetary rewards
- Ferrari CEO Benedetto Vigna notes that patent recognition creates a sense of gratification and ownership

**Multi-Dimensional Rewards.** Octopus Organizations look at rewards across results, skills, behaviors, and both individual and team contributions. Product development teams benefit from group rewards focused on learning and iteration rather than hitting specific metrics. For small, highly interdependent teams, team-based or hybrid rewards outperform individual incentives.

**Personality-Aware Recognition.** Effective rewards for introverts include private recognition (thank-you notes) or self-directed professional development opportunities, rather than public celebrations that appeal primarily to extroverts.

**Equitable Base Pay.** Rather than using variable compensation as a motivational lever, Octopus Organizations establish equitable and consistent base pay separated from gameable performance metrics. When employees perceive equity, they engage in autotelic experiences -- finding inherent satisfaction in their work.

## Key Principles

- Incentives are forward-looking carrots; rewards are retrospective recognition -- they are not the same thing
- Intrinsic motivation (purpose, autonomy, mastery) is harder for competitors to match than pay raises
- Well-designed rewards are equitable, connected to direct achievements, recognize ownership, and value deep thinking over "busyness"
- Reward systems must evolve as rapidly as work itself changes
- Align rewards with leadership principles (customer centricity, ownership, speed of learning)
- Regularly audit who gets promoted and recognized to ensure alignment with stated values

## Real-World Examples

- **Zappos**: "WoW Awards" for extraordinary customer service or demonstration of values
- **Southwest Airlines**: "Heroes of the Heart" celebrating behind-the-scenes departments
- **McDonald's**: Circle of Excellence for cross-functional teams delivering businesswide impacts
- **Nike (Michael Newton)**: Company-wide recognition for customer value creation, more impactful than monetary rewards
- **Ferrari (Benedetto Vigna)**: Patent recognition creates gratification and ownership
- **Lego Group**: Family-friendly policies aligned with brand values accounting for employees' different life stages
- **Netflix**: High degrees of freedom, unlimited vacation days, no formal expense policies
- **Airbnb**: "Live and Work Anywhere" policy -- work from anywhere up to ninety days per year with travel allowance
- **Amazon**: Recognition points convertible into gift cards or tangible items

## Practical Takeaways

1. Broaden recognition methods: company-wide platforms, storytelling (blogs, video series, social media) showcasing teams making remarkable impact
2. Institute peer-to-peer recognition budgets (karma tokens, gift cards, team lunch funds) for immediate, direct spot rewards
3. Design context-specific rewards: weight product team rewards toward customer impact over delivery speed; balance sales revenue targets with customer success
4. Make purpose visible with dashboards showing how work contributes to broader purpose
5. Include at least one team-based objective in every reward or incentive structure
6. Broadcast rewards across organizational boundaries with attribution tracking for successful initiatives
