## The Antipattern

Tin Man organizations choose to tackle the more achievable parts of challenges first and focus on visible progress rather than validating that the hardest parts are achievable. The results are costly delays discovering critical roadblocks, false signs of progress, and missed opportunities to learn and pivot strategy.

## Why It Happens

Astro Teller uses a vivid image: if you wanted a monkey sitting on a ten-foot pedestal to recite Shakespeare, where would you begin? The right answer is to train the monkey first. But "a lot of work, weirdly, goes into the pedestal early on at a lot of companies -- because there's so much pressure to get rewarded for having done a good job. 'Hey, nice pedestal!' At some point the boss is going to pop by and ask for a status update -- and you want to be able to show off something other than a long list of reasons why teaching a monkey to talk is really, really hard."

The temptation to start with the pedestal is natural:
- **Immediate gratification** from visible "wins" that boost short-term morale
- **Showcasing to leaders** who prefer presentations highlighting concrete achievements
- **Resource constraints** that put teams off complex problems requiring significant research
- **Comfort zone** of solving familiar problems where risk of blame is lower

These are not moral failings but byproducts of the Tin Man's need to see progress. Early wins look good on quarterly reports and justify budgets. Incentive structures reward productivity over curiosity and learning. Corporate speak even lauds this: "going after the low-hanging fruit first."

Conversely, tackling hard problems involves ambiguity, uncertainty, and high potential for failure -- daunting in risk-averse cultures that reward progress. Limited bandwidth, budgets, and talent create pressure to focus on quickly "solvable" issues.

## The Octopus Way

**Apply Curiosity to the Hardest Things First.** That is where understanding of risks and learning comes from. As Mark Twain put it: "Eat a live frog first thing in the morning and nothing worse will happen to you the rest of the day."

**Get to "No" Faster.** The faster you figure out your monkey cannot learn Shakespeare, the less time, money, and effort you have sunk, making it easier to quit. Building pedestals spends resources on things that get you no closer to figuring out whether you can achieve your goal. Nothing else matters if you do not solve the hard thing first.

**The Foghorn Example.** At Alphabet X, the Foghorn team developed a way to turn seawater into carbon-neutral liquid fuel. Their "pedestal" was the fuel-generation technology. Their "monkey" was cost-competitiveness. When they realized the monkey was unlikely to work, they called it quits to free resources for ideas more likely to succeed. Though rational, letting go of something the team poured their hearts into was undeniably difficult.

**The Podcast Analogy.** Astro illustrates with a simple example: given one year to establish a massively successful podcast, you could spend it all building followership, creating episodes, and marketing (the pedestal). Or you could spend the first month brainstorming unusual ideas, three months testing them cheaply, two months investing in the top ideas, leaving six months to deliver and scale. Less early satisfaction but more likely to succeed.

**Intellectual Honesty.** When you take ownership of a project and really want it to work, it feels strange to spend the first few months doing the hard thing that might tell you to let go. But most value comes from first proving viability. Paradoxically, tackling the hardest things first with minimal investment and greatest honesty is less risky than grabbing low-hanging fruit.

For people to be open about challenges, they need emotional and financial rewards. If a team knows that honesty about challenges will not get recognition and will damage future efforts, they will end up working secretly on things still likely to fail, just more slowly.

**Benefits of This Approach.** Octopus Organizations develop solutions with broader and unexpected applications (Project Loon's balloon lasers became an earth-based communication system), develop unique capabilities setting them apart from competitors, and attract and retain top talent with meaningful and challenging work.

## Key Principles

- #MonkeyFirst: always start with the hardest part of the project
- Nothing else matters if you do not solve the hard thing first
- False progress on pedestals is worse than no progress -- it consumes resources while leaving the core question unanswered
- Tackling the hardest things first with minimal investment is paradoxically less risky
- Reward intellectual honesty, vulnerability, and "monkey spotting"
- Beware of teams working secretly on things they know will not make it

## Real-World Examples

- **Alphabet X / Astro Teller**: #MonkeyFirst principle, Foghorn project (seawater to fuel), Wing premortem, podcast analogy
- **Waymo / Larry Page**: "Larry 1000 test" -- get a car to one thousand self-driven miles without intervention to face the monkey problems
- **Project Loon**: Balloon-based connectivity that pivoted into earth-based communication when the original "monkey" proved insurmountable
- **Mark Twain**: "Eat a live frog first thing in the morning"

## Practical Takeaways

1. Set ambitious goals that force teams to face the monkey first -- get projects in contact with the physical world ASAP (like Waymo's "Larry 1000 test")
2. Cultivate intellectual honesty: celebrate stopping projects when hard problems prove too challenging, with public recognition and bonuses
3. Change project review culture: for first several check-ins on risky projects, forbid polished slides or "pedestal" progress -- require demonstration of messiest, most incomplete progress on the single hardest part
4. Engage teams' desire for recognition of creativity: ask "What are the things that could derail us? What are we not seeing?" and thank contributors
5. Reframe monkeys as learning: ask "What are the biggest learning opportunities?" and "What are the key unknowns?" rather than emphasizing the challenge
6. Use "What if?" questions for scenario planning: "What if our key assumption is wrong?" "What if the technical challenge proves harder?" "What if a competitor takes a different approach?"
7. Consider a lighthearted "Golden Monkey Award" (or similar) to reinforce monkey-spotting behavior
