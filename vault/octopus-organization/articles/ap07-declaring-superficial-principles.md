## The Antipattern

Le-Brun and Werner describe organizations that adopt the form of leadership principles without understanding or committing to their function. They create or adopt aspirational statements that sound impressive but are decorations -- "beautiful ones, yes, but hardly actionable and often forgettable."

The authors share the story of "Northern Frontier Wealth" (a real situation with disguised names) where CEO "Sam" launched a "Values Journey." A twelve-person task force spent six months using AI-driven sentiment analysis of other organizations' principles to produce a 115-slide methodology deck behind seven principles including gems like "Be profitably ethical," "Deliver excellence through synergistic teamwork," "Think like an owner, act like a leader, work like a startup," and "Practice mindful aggressive growth." The rollout included a principles-themed escape room, a three-hour webinar, and gold leaf-embossed booklets. Six months later, 84% of employees could not recall a single principle. "Be profitably ethical" survived only as a cynical catchphrase. Two years later, a new CEO quietly retired them.

Three types of failing principles:

1. **Cosmetic principles**: Not woven into behaviors, decision-making, incentive structures, or hiring. Employees receive mixed signals -- the principles say one thing, but how work gets done says another.
2. **Generic principles**: So aspirational as to be unattainable, offering no guidance for real-world dilemmas.
3. **Self-evident principles**: Ideas that should not need stating or have no logical opposite, like "working ethically" or "doing the right thing for customers."

## Why It Happens

This antipattern typically emerges with new leadership wanting to put their stamp on the organization, at the beginning of a major strategic shift, or when organizations see a competitor whose principles have gained notice. The fundamental dissonance is the pride leaders take in principles versus their actual use. Leaders create an artifact -- a document, video, plaque -- and hold activities to celebrate that document, when they should be thinking of principles as habits and routines.

The expectation of having principles traces to Thomas Peters and Robert Waterman's observation in *In Search of Excellence* that exceptional companies had explicit shared values. But most people cannot recall their own organization's principles, let alone use them to guide work.

## The Octopus Way

Octopus Organizations understand principles as the manifestation of values that serve as a compass enabling decentralized decision-making while preserving integrity and coherence. Their principles are **useful, lived, and living**:

**Useful principles** offer context and practical descriptions, brought to life through examples and stories. Amazon's "Leaders are right, a lot" is often misinterpreted, but its description explains: "They have strong judgment and good instincts. They seek diverse perspectives and work to disconfirm their beliefs." The behaviors that produce this outcome are enumerated in the principle itself.

**Lived principles** are demonstrated through consistent leadership behavior. Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella role models "learn from others" by talking to other CEOs every day. Netflix's "The Dream Team" principle is concise and clear: they model themselves on a professional sports team, not a family -- "Families are about unconditional love. They can also be dysfunctional... Professional sports teams, on the other hand, focus on performance and picking the right person for every position." Netflix adds callouts tied to principles like "Selflessness," detailing expected behaviors.

**Living principles** embrace productive tensions and evolve over time. Amazon reinforces this by adding "unless you know better" whenever principles are shown or discussed. Dynafit CEO Benedikt Bohm sends handwritten Christmas cards with principles on the back and describes the work as "permanently hard... You have to spar with your leaders about principles... Real adoption requires giving people time to reflect and challenge the principles."

Octopus Organizations also embrace **tension between principles** -- a "bias for action" might conflict with "diving deep." Rather than eliminating the tension, leaders use it to spark deeper discussion and more nuanced decision-making.

The result: principles attract employees who share those values while deterring those who don't. At least a third of potential employees use them as an acceptance criterion for jobs.

## Key Principles

1. Principles are habits and routines, not artifacts to celebrate
2. Useful principles are concise, behavior-driven, and brought to life with examples
3. Lived principles require consistent leadership role modeling
4. Living principles evolve and embrace productive tension
5. The distinctness comes not from unique principles but from the unique combination and its connection to purpose and strategy
6. Principles enable decentralized decision-making by creating a shared operating system

## Real-World Examples

- **Northern Frontier Wealth (disguised)**: Six-month, AI-assisted principles development yielded seven unmemorable principles; 84% of employees could not recall one within six months.
- **Amazon**: "Leaders are right, a lot" includes behavioral descriptions and is reinforced with "unless you know better" to keep principles living. Principles are embedded in hiring, performance reviews, and daily decision-making.
- **Netflix**: "The Dream Team" principle uses the professional sports team analogy with specific behavioral callouts like "Selflessness."
- **Microsoft (Satya Nadella)**: Role models "learn from others" by daily conversations with other CEOs.
- **Dynafit (Benedikt Bohm)**: Handwritten Christmas cards with principles; constant sparring with leaders about principles.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Codify exemplar behaviors**: Identify a dozen employees who embody desired behaviors, interview and shadow them, then craft principles from those insights.
- **Connect principles to purpose**: Each principle should directly support the organization's purpose; discard any without a clear connection.
- **Integrate into business processes**: Structure performance reviews around how employees demonstrated key principles; create specific interview questions for each principle.
- **Bring principles to life**: Monthly leader videos on a principle in action; celebrate employees who role model principles at all-hands.
- **Adopt the language**: Use principle language in everyday interactions -- thanking people by calling out a specific principle, using principle language to disagree respectfully.
- **Sample effectiveness regularly**: Poll employees about their favorite principle and how they use it day-to-day, focusing on usefulness rather than compliance.
- **Practice principle-based decision-making**: For important decisions, explicitly ask which principles apply, how each option aligns, where tensions arise, and document the reasoning.
