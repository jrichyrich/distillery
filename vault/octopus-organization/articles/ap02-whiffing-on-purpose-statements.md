# Antipattern #2: Whiffing on Purpose Statements

*An authentic, specific, practical purpose improves performance.*

## The Antipattern

Organizations use buzzword-laden purpose or vision statements that lack conviction, authenticity, or meaning to inspire performance. The results are strategic drift and indecision, a culture of compliance not contribution, employee disengagement, and erosion of trust and brand integrity.

Le-Brun and Werner share real examples (disguised to protect the guilty):

- "The future, powered by generative AI" -- *Thanks for the answer, what was the question?!*
- "Advancing what matters" -- *the alternative being ...*
- "Tomorrow starts today" -- *er, no, it doesn't*
- "Undisputed marketplace leadership" -- *an aspiration, but not a purpose*
- "To leverage synergies and optimize core competencies to achieve impactful stakeholder value in a dynamic global environment" -- *if we work at decoding this magnificently dense jargon, we think it means "to run a business"*

The Cheshire Cat's wisdom from *Alice in Wonderland* applies: "If you don't know where you're going, it doesn't matter which path you take."

## Why It Happens

- **Contractual mindset**: Tin Man environments have taught executives to see the relationship with employees as contractual -- both sides trying to minimize expense and effort for maximum output. Leaders see incentives and controls as the prime motivators, not purpose.
- **The self-fulfilling prophecy**: Business school training and workplace experiences lead leaders to downgrade the importance of our innate need for meaning.
- **Marketing exercise**: Many leaders see purpose statements as marketing for Wall Street and PR, not as operational tools.
- **Lack of deep work**: Leaders have not done the hard work of actually determining their organization's purpose on a resonant intellectual and emotional level.
- **Convenient purpose** (Ranjay Gulati's term from *Deep Purpose*): Superficial statements designed to "sound good and feel right" that fail to prompt meaningful action or trade-offs.

The data is damning: Daniel Coyle found in *The Culture Code* that despite 64% of executives believing employees knew their purpose statement, in truth it was just **2%**. One survey found 52% of leaders acknowledged their purpose statement was seen more as a brand and marketing play. Nearly one-third of employees think their organizations lie about their purpose. Gallup reported only 21% of employees globally are engaged, costing 9% of global GDP annually in low engagement. Scott Galloway showed an inverse relationship between the degree of bullshit in purpose statements and long-term share price.

## The Octopus Way

Octopus Organizations create meaningful purpose statements that are **specific, authentic, and provide clear direction**. They answer three foundational questions:

1. What is your reason for existing?
2. What value are you giving your customers?
3. Why is your organization uniquely capable of providing that?

**Purpose resonates intellectually and emotionally.** As Daniel Pink explains in *Drive*, humans have a deep need for mastery, autonomy, and purpose. Ranjay Gulati terms this "moral motivation" -- employees driven by a desire to contribute to something meaningful. The apocryphal story of JFK asking a NASA janitor about his role, and the janitor replying "Well, Mr. President, I'm helping put a man on the moon," captures this perfectly.

**Purpose is simple, honest, inspirational, and jargon-free:**

- **Starbucks**: "To inspire and nurture the human spirit -- one person, one cup, and one neighborhood at a time." (Focuses on human connection)
- **Mastercard**: "Connecting and powering an inclusive, digital economy that benefits everyone, everywhere."
- **Merck**: "We use the power of leading-edge science to save and improve lives around the world." (Led them to withdraw profitable drugs that did not improve lives, and to freely distribute ivermectin to cure river blindness in impoverished areas)
- **IKEA**: "Create a better everyday life for the many people." Founder Ingvar Kamprad established IKEA to serve those with modest means. He stayed true even when competitors pressured him to raise prices, instead bringing production in-house and seeking new manufacturing sources.

**Purpose as transformation catalyst.** Dame Julia Hoggett tapped into the 300-year history and purpose of the London Stock Exchange, which needed to adapt how its job was done. This created a transformation "pull" by making it clear that "not changing would be illogical." The proposed changes seemed natural to fulfill the purpose.

**Purpose is brought to life** through all communications, watercooler chats, shareholder reports, staff one-on-ones, and by leaders who arm themselves with talking points on the purpose to use regularly.

## Key Principles

- **"Just cause"** (Simon Sinek): A noble, often customer-centric or society-centric reason for existing. "People don't buy what you do; people buy why you do it."
- **"Profit in service of what?"** (Dame Julia Hoggett): Profit is a consequence of serving the cause, not the cause itself
- **"Growing the pie"** (Alex Edmans): Companies that deliver genuine value to society ultimately deliver greater long-term profits
- **Deep vs. convenient purpose** (Ranjay Gulati): Deep purpose is not just aspirational but operational, informing strategy and willingly embracing trade-offs
- **Intrinsic > extrinsic motivation** (Daniel Pink): Mastery, autonomy, and purpose are more powerful than bonuses or job titles

## Real-World Examples

- **Netflix** -- Reed Hastings in *No Rules Rules* describes a manager who made an "awful" decision that was actually sensible given his information; the root cause was insufficient context about overarching purpose
- **Merck** -- Purpose led to withdrawing profitable drugs that did not improve lives and freely distributing ivermectin for river blindness
- **IKEA** -- Ingvar Kamprad kept prices low despite competitor pressure, staying true to serving those with modest means
- **London Stock Exchange** -- Dame Julia Hoggett used the 300-year institutional purpose to create transformation pull, making not changing seem illogical
- **Starbucks, Mastercard** -- Examples of purpose statements that are simple, authentic, and connected to origin stories

## Practical Takeaways

1. **Diagnose your current purpose.** Test along five dimensions: Inspiration (what emotions does it evoke?), Connection (does it make people feel part of something meaningful?), Simplicity and memorability (can people recall it?), Authenticity (does it reflect actual behavior and history?), Specificity and guidance (does it guide strategic choices and trade-offs?).
2. **Rediscover or refine your purpose.** Explore founding ideals, unique capabilities, and genuine needs of customers and society. Involve diverse voices in the discovery process. Aim for "deep purpose" -- not just aspirational but operational.
3. **Bring your purpose to life.** What story will you tell that illustrates how your purpose is meaningful? How does it connect to your organization's origin story?
4. **Make it a tie-breaker.** Use the purpose to make a difficult decision. Talk about how it guided the choice. Encourage leaders to weave purpose into team goals, trade-offs, recognition, and coaching.
5. **Change the language if needed.** If "purpose" and "mission" get in the way, create a pride or ambition statement. What makes you proud to work here? What ambition do you hold?
