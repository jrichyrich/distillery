# Antipattern #34: Segregating Technology

## The Antipattern

Technology and data are treated differently from any other major business discipline, separated by language and expertise that reinforce a division between "the business" and the technology group that supports "the business." Le-Brun and Werner compare this to the Sumerian legend of King Enmerkar, whose oral messages relayed across intermediaries and languages became distorted, turning a straightforward trade negotiation into prolonged conflict.

The authors demonstrate the problem viscerally: Phil might say "Paying down our technical debt by decomposing monolithic applications into microservices..." -- and many leaders outside IT would hear complex jargon that feels annoyingly not to the point. Yet Phil could simply say: "We're simplifying our systems: breaking down large, complex software programs into smaller, more manageable ones, like renovating a house one room at a time."

Ask the average executive whether they understand managing money and they'll scoff. Ask them if they understand AI and they'll say "I understand it's important" but happily cede understanding beyond that to technologists. Less than 12 percent of board members have a technology background. Digital fluency among C-suite roles ranges from 8 to 23 percent.

**Tin Man results:**
- Complexity and technical debt that erodes performance
- Tendency to wastefully chase tech trends
- Failure to translate tech investment into customer impact

## Why It Happens

The root causes lie deeper than jargon:

- **Legacy hierarchies creating power distance**: IT is relegated to a support function rather than a strategic driver. Leaders who ascended in a pre-digital era lack nuanced understanding of technology's potential, preventing tech from getting "a seat at the table."
- **Misaligned incentives and metrics**: Business units are measured on financial metrics (revenue, costs); IT is evaluated on operational metrics (uptime, system availability). This incentivizes business leaders to prioritize bottom-line impact while IT focuses on stability at the expense of innovation.
- **A clash of time horizons**: Business drives change through projects (defined start and end) while IT manages evolving product portfolios requiring continuous support. This creates misalignment in funding, lifecycle management, team structure, and fundamental focus.
- **Process over people**: Stage-gated approvals and formal handoffs between "the business" and "IT" create bottlenecks and reinforce separation.
- **Self-siloing by technologists**: Tech leaders insist on jargon (meshes, bimodal operating models, abstraction layers, DevSecOps) and then frown when non-technology colleagues look confused.

The consequences are profound: useless requirements built on misunderstandings, more technology than needed, shadow IT creating security issues, existing applications degrading unused, and new technologies poorly explained.

## The Octopus Way

Octopus leaders recognize that technology fluency is a required skill, just as understanding finance and customers is.

**Key Octopus behaviors:**

- **Champion proactive education.** Go beyond passive briefings: hands-on sessions solving real business problems, structured immersion experiences visiting tech-forward companies, and reverse mentoring pairing technically fluent employees with senior leaders.
- **Coach technologists to communicate effectively.** There is "nothing smart about making it complicated," as one supermarket's chief retail and technology officer said. Use metaphors and storytelling to bridge the gap. Stephen Brozovich uses the "currency of leadership" to connect technology to what resonates with individual leaders. One executive demonstrated AI's potential by showing how it could improve a leader's golf game.
- **Redefine ownership and cocreation.** Flip the usual process: get business owners to participate in debating problems and tradeoffs alongside technologists, considering benefit optimization, cost reduction, and technical stability. Ultimately dismantle silos in favor of persistent, cross-functional product teams (Amazon's "two-pizza teams").
- **Use shared metrics.** Focus on business outcomes (customer lifetime value, speed to market, market share) that both technology and business units contribute to and are accountable for.
- **Expect leaders to show curiosity.** Leaders should proactively read, experiment with technology, and ask the simple question: "Can you help me understand...?"

Research shows organizations with digitally fluent leadership significantly outperform peers in revenue growth, valuations, and margins.

## Key Principles

1. **Technology fluency is a leadership requirement** -- not a specialist's skill
2. **No sides** -- phrases like "the business and IT" are silo-building language that should be actively discouraged
3. **Shared metrics bridge divides** -- when technology and business are measured on the same outcomes, alignment follows
4. **Communication is bidirectional** -- technologists must speak the lingua franca of the business, and business leaders must develop genuine technological curiosity

## Real-World Examples

- **McDonald's**: SVP Whitney McGinnis starts every townhall with a "state of the business" update connecting her technology team's work to business impact, ensuring technologists feel they CAN impact the business
- **Amazon**: Two-pizza teams dismantle silos into persistent cross-functional product teams
- **Walmart**: Push to make floor staff tech-savvy
- **Capital One**: Training for non-technical stakeholders
- **Supermarket CTO**: Flipped the usual process by getting business owners to participate in debating problems and tradeoffs alongside technologists

## Practical Takeaways

1. **Establish reverse mentoring** -- pair technically savvy employees with senior executives; pair technologists with non-tech leaders for communication coaching
2. **Schedule regular technology immersion days** -- 45-minute slots on boardroom agendas to explore tech topics, showcase prototypes, guide leaders through writing AI prompts
3. **Create technology feedback loops** -- regular surveys where business users evaluate the clarity of tech communications
4. **Build tech fundamentals curriculum** -- cloud basics, AI concepts, cybersecurity awareness, data literacy, product management; consider making technology fluency a criterion for advancement
5. **Develop a common vocabulary** -- create a "living glossary" translating tech terms to business impact
6. **Eat your own dog food** -- assign executives ownership of tech products for three months; require them to use the products and participate in development
7. **Communicate weekly** -- one-page updates on tech topics and how they support customer and business value
8. **Implement cross-functional pilot teams** -- give them business outcome missions and shared metrics; wrap a protective layer including representatives from finance and HR
