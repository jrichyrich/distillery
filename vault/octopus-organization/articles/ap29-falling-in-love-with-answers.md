## The Antipattern

Tin Man organizations rush to solve challenges with urgency to show progress before deeply understanding the problem or validating the solution, rewarding action over understanding. The results are poor solutions based on incomplete understanding of root causes, ineffective products and services, and missed opportunities to create impact.

## Why It Happens

Solutions are seductive. Spending time on solutions feels more productive than time spent trying to understand causes. As organizational psychologist David Hofmann describes, this seduction leads to rushed decision-making focused on "What should we do?" reinforced by cultures that favor action over questions. It creates over-reliance on past experiences (where the brain first goes to solve problems) and competitive advocacy as teams try to get their solution to win rather than collaborate on defining the problem.

Once we fall in love with solutions, biases prevent contrary information from interfering: confirmation bias crowds out contradictory data, the availability heuristic favors easily-recalled ideas, anchoring bias favors ideas heard first, and the "neighborhood of symptom" rule makes us try to solve problems where we see symptoms rather than asking why the symptom exists. A missed sales target prompts investigation of the sales department, then pricing and product quality, but rarely extends to questioning fundamental market or customer assumptions.

Tin Man organizations' focus on productivity means solutions become hard currency -- a way to show activity. The "bring me solutions" mentality does not ask the right questions or seek root causes. Without clear understanding, organizations waste time, money, and energy on features and products their customers do not need. Impatience, arrogance, misapplied experience, and desire to get going are traits of organizations that regularly fall in love with bad solutions.

**General Magic** exemplifies this. The 1990s Silicon Valley company, packed with former Apple geniuses like Bill Atkinson and Andy Hertzfeld, created the Magic Link -- a device with phone, touchscreen, email, games, downloadable apps, and animated emojis, years before the iPhone. The press described it as "absolutely, flat-out, ahead-of-its-time amazing." It sold only about three thousand devices, mostly to family and friends. The failure was not engineering -- Magic Link was a technical marvel. It was a failure to be curious about what customers wanted. They skipped user research and only started testing once engineering was done. Not long after, Palm emerged with a successful PDA that simply solved a customer problem: "How can I bring my Rolodex with me on the go?"

## The Octopus Way

**Fall in Love with the Problem.** The Octopus leader makes sure the why drives the what, not vice versa. As the classic formulation goes: "People don't buy drills, they buy holes."

**Deliberately Slow Down Problem-Solving.** Multiple forms of slowdown exist:
- Amazon's **Working Backwards** (PR-FAQ) requires teams to write a press release before building anything
- Toyota's **Five Whys** method traces root causes systematically
- **Problem-solution pairing**: presenting solutions only with clear, specific customer stories prevents designing for the wrong customer group

**Disney's MagicBand.** When facing declining performance, instead of cutting costs or raising prices, Disney asked "How else could we fix this?" and focused on reducing time guests spent in line. They learned from a Tokyo pharmacy using radio-frequency identification technology, turning it into MagicBand. Guests got more time to play and spend. It increased quarterly results by 3 percent without raising gate prices -- one of the biggest single revenue-generating hits in Disney park history.

**Spotify's Echo Chamber Discovery.** When noticing declining user engagement, instead of rushing to add features, Spotify spent six weeks mapping user journeys, revealing the root cause: their recommendation algorithm was creating echo chambers -- a systemic issue that quick-fix feature additions would have missed.

**Netflix's Content Cache Insight.** Facing video buffering complaints, rather than immediately investing in faster servers, systematic analysis revealed that partnering with ISPs to install local content caches would be far more effective.

**Quick Path to "No."** Alphabet X's Astro Teller observed that a team saying "We know what the right thing to do is" raises red flags. A team that says "We're probably wrong" has a better chance of turning the loop faster. Instead of "How quickly can we get to yes?" ask "How efficiently can we get to no?"

**Soliciting Problems, Not Solutions.** When Anita Krohn Traaseth took over Norway's government innovation efforts, she specifically solicited problems through "speed dates" with employees across fourteen offices, gathering insights about bottlenecks before considering solutions. As former Starbucks International president Howard Behar said: "Leaders sometimes think they need all the right answers. It's the opposite -- they need to ask the right questions."

## Key Principles

- The why must drive the what, not vice versa
- Delay commitment, not exploration
- Seeking understanding is slower at first but more open-ended and ultimately more efficient
- "Don't bring me problems; bring me solutions" is a dangerous mantra -- flip it
- Meetings should be clearly delineated as problem-seeking, problem-solving, or solution advocacy, with different facilitation techniques for each
- The more exciting an idea seems, the longer to wait before committing to it (delayed intuition)

## Real-World Examples

- **General Magic**: Technical marvel that failed because it solved no customer problem; Palm succeeded by solving a simple one
- **Disney MagicBand**: Slowing down to reframe the problem led to one of the biggest revenue hits in Disney park history
- **Spotify**: Six weeks mapping user journeys revealed algorithmic echo chambers, not a feature gap
- **Netflix**: Systematic analysis pointed to ISP content caches rather than faster servers
- **Alphabet X (Astro Teller)**: "How efficiently can we get to no?" rather than rushing to yes
- **Anita Krohn Traaseth (Norway)**: "Speed dates" with employees to gather problems before solutions
- **Howard Behar (Starbucks)**: "Leaders need to ask the right questions, not have all the right answers"
- **Amazon**: Working Backwards process and customer opportunity statement rubric
- **Toyota**: Five Whys method for root-cause analysis

## Practical Takeaways

1. Mandate clear problem/opportunity statements: "The current process for X results in Y inefficiency, costing Z. We need to find a way to achieve W outcome."
2. Ask better questions: "How might we?" "What core assumptions are we making?" "What would need to be true for the proposed solution to fail spectacularly?"
3. Practice delayed intuition: research and prototype before committing to exciting ideas
4. Practice problem-solution pairing: require concrete customer stories showing why a proposed solution is the right one
5. Employ discovery techniques: customer personas, empathy maps, journey diagrams, jobs-to-be-done interviews, prototypes, Eric von Hippel's "lead user" approach
6. Demand multiple possible solutions before moving forward, using divergent thinking exercises (Crazy 8s, SCAMPER, brainwriting)
7. Avoid habituation: use Gemba walks, "fresh eyes" reviews, "Day in the Life" studies, and "negative brainstorming"
8. Implement continuous discovery habits: weekly short customer interviews and rapid usability tests as part of regular workflow
