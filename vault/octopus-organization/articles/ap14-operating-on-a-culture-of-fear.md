## The Antipattern

Organizations create environments where individuals hesitate to own tasks, voice concerns, share ideas, or admit mistakes for fear of negative consequences. This leads to decreased engagement, missed opportunities, stifled innovation, and avoidance of calculated risks. Le-Brun and Werner place this antipattern first in the ownership section because psychological safety is the prerequisite -- no one will claim ownership if they believe they will be penalized for doing so.

Psychological safety, as the authors define it drawing on Amy Edmondson's research, is an individual's belief about whether it is worth taking interpersonal risks without fear of humiliation, rejection, or punishment. It differs from trust (built between individuals ahead of time) in that it manifests in group settings, in the moment, as a person assesses whether to take a risk.

## Why It Happens

The roots of fear cultures are deep and self-reinforcing:

- **Historical trauma**: Past incidents where openness led to retribution -- even unintentionally -- teach people that speaking up is dangerous. Once someone has been "bitten," the lesson persists.
- **Misaligned processes and metrics**: Formal or informal systems that reward supporting the status quo directly contradict proclamations of safety.
- **Lack of clarity**: Employees do not understand why or how their honest opinions are valued.
- **No positive precedents**: A dearth of visible examples where "speaking up" led to a good outcome for all involved.
- **Neurological wiring**: Psychological safety is emotional, rooted deep in the brain, and will override nuanced thinking when a threat is sensed.
- **Leader skepticism**: Some leaders dismiss psychological safety as "touchy-feely" or believe people should "toughen up," not realizing this stance produces the very timidity they despise.

The Nokia case (late 2000s) illustrates the pattern powerfully. Despite then-CEO Stephen Elop's explicit efforts to create psychological safety through "Nokia Conversations" and open feedback channels, many employees who saw warning signs of Nokia's smartphone struggles remained silent. Leadership invited feedback; employees refused the invitation because the emotional calculus of risk still pointed to silence.

## The Octopus Way

Octopus Organizations take a multi-layered approach to building genuine psychological safety:

**Understanding what psychological safety is not:**
- Being soft or lowering expectations
- Treating every idea as equally valid
- Avoiding tough conversations or tolerating repeated mistakes
- Inclusion for the sake of inclusion

**Recognizing the signs of healthy vulnerability:**
- Openness: spontaneous contributions, thinking aloud, sharing partially formed ideas
- Playfulness: laughter that reduces stress and fear (Rich Hua, founder of Amazon's EQ@Amazon program, aims to get teams laughing in the first five minutes of meetings)
- Empathy: anger and frustration addressed through perspective-taking
- Inquisitiveness: open-ended questions to elucidate thinking
- Reflection: comfort with silence to pause and think
- Gratitude: honesty is rewarded and recognized

**Modeling vulnerability from the top:** Leaders showcase their own mistakes and doubts. Alphabet X CEO Astro Teller: "If you really want people to be unleashed, you need to first put down all your armor, take off all your masks, and then you need to start rewarding people when they do it."

**Eliminating brilliant jerks:** Nothing weakens the willingness to speak up more than a person who poisons the environment with arrogance, disrespect, or intimidation -- no matter how smart or experienced they are.

**Thinking locally:** Amy Edmondson told the authors, "There is no such thing as an 'organization.' There are groups in the organization. Some have higher psychological safety, some lower. Psychological safety is a local phenomenon." Practices should be deployed at the team level.

**Nurturing inclusivity:** Tanuja Randery CBE (former Schneider Electric President and AWS EMEA VP) observed that "when a junior or diverse talent offers a perspective and finds it dismissed, the immediate loss isn't just that single insight; it's the effect on future innovation across the team."

LEGO nurtures psychological safety through its "leadership playground" culture, encouraging employees to be curious, brave, and focused -- the attributes found in children at a playground.

## Key Principles

1. Psychological safety is always an individual's decision -- you cannot mandate it, only create the conditions for it
2. Vulnerability reinforces safety; the ability to say "I was wrong" or "I don't know" creates positive reinforcement
3. Trust is a precursor -- people need to know others' intentions come from a place of personal care
4. Even the Navy SEALs prize psychological safety deeply, debunking the "soft" stereotype
5. The real challenge is not understanding what to do but the relentless dedication to doing it when pressure mounts

## Practical Takeaways

- **Ask "What am I missing?"** -- make this the go-to question in meetings to model intellectual humility. Amy Edmondson suggests "What are you worrying about?"
- **Normalize and learn from issues** by conducting blameless postmortems and celebrating "intelligent failures" that come from ambitious attempts
- **"Call in" rather than "call out"** -- approach mistakes privately and with curiosity to reduce fear of public shaming
- **Build inclusive meeting practices** with turn-taking protocols, rotating roles, and drawing out quieter voices
- **Provide multiple contribution channels** beyond speaking up in meetings (e.g., Ovo Energy's former Chief People Officer Kim Atherton advocates anonymous voting mechanisms for introverts)
- **Regularly assess psychological safety** through surveys and discussions, but heed Edmondson's advice: "Psychological safety is a means to an end, measuring performance is the goal"
- **Start meetings with optional check-in questions** such as "What's one thing that energized you this week?" or "On a scale of 1-10, how present can you be today and why?"
