## The Antipattern

Le-Brun and Werner observe that the word "priority" was singular for centuries, signifying the first thing. The plural arrived with industrial-era management -- and has been relentlessly pluralized since. They worked with an international fashion retailer whose transformation program had four hundred priorities alongside other strategic imperatives and business-as-usual activities.

The Tin Man behavior: pursuing an overwhelming number of projects, ideas, and initiatives while avoiding difficult choices. The results are plummeting productivity from thinly spread resources, a stressed and confused workforce, and constant firefighting.

## Why It Happens

The authors identify multiple drivers:

- **Additive culture**: Organizations add priorities as if they had unlimited resources, but every addition subtracts energy from every other priority.
- **Leadership churn**: At Yahoo!, priorities sprawled across search, email, news, social networking, advertising, e-commerce, fantasy sports, productivity apps, job recruiting, and gaming -- shifting with each leadership change, weakly linked to core objectives.
- **Internal politics**: Influence, not strategic merit, dictates resource allocation.
- **Tyranny of the urgent**: Immediate demands overshadow long-term investment. Nearly half of tech budgets are consumed by business-as-usual "priorities" that are not really priorities.
- **Ghost deprioritization**: Leaders say they are deprioritizing something, but resources are not reallocated, and people are still informally expected to work on the "deprioritized" item.
- **100% utilization trap**: Tin Man organizations fixate on assigning 100% resource allocation to "priority work," blocking opportunities to innovate or automate low-value work.

Studies show perhaps only 2% of employees can name their company's top priorities. A more optimistic study found 25% of managers could list three of their firm's top five. Multitasking across many priorities imposes a cognitive tax -- each context switch erodes time, increases mistakes, and damages learning capacity. Over 60% of employees struggle with time and energy for their core job; overwhelmed individuals are 3.5 times more likely to struggle with innovation and strategic thinking.

## The Octopus Way

Octopus Organizations champion ruthless prioritization, understanding that focus is a force multiplier. General Sir Patrick Sanders of the British Army: "We will need to suppress our additive culture and guard against the 'tyranny of and.'"

- **"Less, but better" philosophy** (Greg McKeown's *Essentialism*): Systematically discern what is essential and eliminate everything else. Companies focusing on a maximum of three priorities are more likely to achieve above-average growth.
- **Strengthen the organizational "no" muscle**: Google's Sundar Pichai says no to good ideas to ensure focus. Warren Buffett: "Really successful people say no to almost everything." Jason Fried and David Heinemeier Hansson (*Rework*): "It's so easy to say yes... Soon, the stack of things you've said yes to grows so tall you can't even see the things you should really be doing."
- **The "Hell, yes" test**: Investments that get everyone around the table fired up are top candidates to be one of your few priorities. If a proposed initiative does not generate enthusiastic support and clear strategic alignment, do not commit.
- **Create a culture where "no" is acceptable**: When saying no, people explain why in terms of existing priorities and strategic goals. Lyft CEO David Risher told his team that any activities beyond ridesharing were up for cutting: "Lyft had become infected with optionality."
- **Tap into teams' insight**: Western Union CIO Ryan Seaman asked teams two questions: "What are you working on you should not be?" and "What should you be working on and why?" Analyzing responses helped halt 60% of active projects.
- **Write objective kill criteria and use stage-gates**: Preset conditions that trigger reevaluation counteract sunk cost fallacy and commitment bias. Companies like 3M, Corning, and PepsiCo embed go/kill decision points in initiatives.
- **Cut initiatives, not costs**: When McDonald's faced difficulties in 2015, rather than eliminating staff, it cut failing and duplicative initiatives, freeing up $500 million and talent. Then it announced just three initiatives: e-commerce for 20,000 restaurants, home delivery for 8,000 restaurants, and reimagined service through self-order kiosks and table service. "Everything changed."
- **Prevent zombie projects**: Set priorities so obviously important and linked to purpose, strategy, and performance evaluation that leaders would not be interested in allowing zombie projects to continue.
- **Communicate incessantly**: The what, the what not, and the why. Alicia Greenwood (CEO, Johannesburg Stock Exchange's Clear business) prompts leaders to clarify "why X was chosen over Y."

## Key Principles

1. If everything is a priority, nothing is
2. Deprioritization is as important a discipline as prioritization
3. Fewer, clearer priorities (maximum of three) correlate with above-average growth
4. Kill criteria defined upfront counteract cognitive biases that prevent stopping
5. Cutting initiatives is better than cutting costs within bloated initiatives
6. Constant communication of priorities -- and non-priorities -- drives commitment

## Real-World Examples

- **Yahoo!**: Priority sprawl across dozens of product lines contributed to loss of market share to Google (which Yahoo! had two opportunities to acquire).
- **International fashion retailer**: Four hundred transformation priorities alongside BAU activities.
- **McDonald's (2015)**: Cut failing initiatives to free $500M and talent, then focused on three clear initiatives that transformed the company.
- **Western Union (Ryan Seaman)**: Two simple questions to teams helped halt 60% of active projects.
- **Lyft (David Risher)**: Declared all activities beyond ridesharing were up for cutting to cure "infection with optionality."
- **3M, Corning, PepsiCo**: Stage-gate processes with go/kill decision points at each gate.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Create a visible "stop doing" list**: Document and display what has been stopped, along with resources freed. Review regularly.
- **Establish kill criteria upfront**: For each new initiative, define clear, assessable criteria and timeframes. Track how many initiatives are scrapped per quarter and celebrate stopping them.
- **Use the "Hell, yes" test**: If a proposed initiative does not generate enthusiastic support, do not commit. Back enthusiasm with objective evaluation against strategic priorities and customer data.
- **Visualize capacity constraints**: Use Kanban boards to make work-in-progress visible and enforce strict limits on concurrent initiatives.
- **Test with smaller-scale versions**: Gather data on trial-size initiatives before full investment.
- **Challenge by default**: Make "Why now?" and "What is the opportunity cost?" the standard response to new initiatives.
