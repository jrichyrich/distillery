## The Antipattern

Organizations give many people a partial say in decisions without any one person having final say or owning outcomes. Le-Brun and Werner use the chicken-and-pig joke as their central metaphor: the chicken contributes (eggs), but the pig commits (ham). Most organizations are full of "chickens" -- people happy to contribute opinions, support consensus, and offer partial approvals -- while few volunteer to be the "pig" who makes tough calls and owns delivery of value.

The results are painfully slow, inconsistent, punted decision-making; stalled and failed initiatives; and paralysis with a blame culture. The authors witnessed a "smart equipment project" at McDonald's that dragged on for seven years as a "zombie project" with no clear owner and no real progress.

## Why It Happens

- **Structural silos**: Incentives are tied to goals within functional silos, not cross-functional outcomes, making end-to-end ownership unappealing
- **Fear of failure**: No one wants to be the pig who becomes ham -- it is safer to be a voice at the table than the owner of a potentially failing initiative
- **Faux ownership**: Leaders claim ownership but never show up (e.g., a COO who told his team he was the ultimate owner of six strategic initiatives yet never attended any steering groups)
- **Support function creep**: Companies allow support functions to make key decisions on major revenue-generating projects rather than actual P&L owners
- **Meeting culture**: Flocks of chickens migrate from meeting to meeting, laying eggs whether needed or not. Meetings cost 24 billion hours annually in the US alone. As Jason Fried and David Heinemeier Hansson note in *Rework*, meetings "are usually about words and abstract concepts, not real things."
- **Expanding stakeholder lists**: Key decisions are delayed because of an ever-expanding list of stakeholders who need to be "in the loop" but none of whom own the decision

## The Octopus Way

**Champion Single-Threaded Leaders (STLs).** An STL (or similar construct like Apple's "Directly Responsible Individual" / DRI) is accountable for delivering a meaningful outcome and making the necessary decisions to achieve it. Former US government CIO Mark Schwartz emphasizes that STLs should not be passive "champions" -- they dive deep into issues, maintain and communicate the vision of success, push every day on progress, and actively remove impediments. Their singular focus enables much faster decision-making.

**Set up STLs for success by:**
- Creating clarity on scope, strategic context, desired outcome, and how it contributes to broader strategy (with tangible metrics)
- Granting genuine authority to decide how work gets done and who to involve (within guardrails)
- Providing coaching and support
- Establishing rapid escalation paths (Amazon encourages fast, early, and "high" escalations)
- Creating a psychologically safe environment for tough decisions
- Aligning incentives to sustainable success of the outcome for long-term ownership thinking

**The act of assigning STLs forces prioritization.** If the organization struggles to appoint someone because they are worried about what that person would be taken away from, then the initiative may not be critical enough.

**The STL model is not riskier than the status quo.** The Tin Man system of diffused responsibility is already incredibly risky and costly (cost of indecision, resource-intensive, slow), just in normalized ways. The STL model introduces more visible and manageable risk with much higher potential for speed, clarity, and delivery. An STL is not an unchecked autocrat -- they have a clear mandate with scope and guardrails.

**Treat consensus-seeking as a red flag for indecision.** Adopt "disagree and commit" -- individuals debate using evidence and anecdotes, but everyone leaves fully committed to the STL's decision. Trust is key: contributors trust the STL will continue to listen to counterpoints and adapt to new evidence.

**Make action the default, not discussion.** Rather than pre-scheduled status meetings where the chicken flock gathers to be "informed" or "consulted," STLs are trusted to get on with the work. Meetings are convened to solve problems together when needed, not scheduled weekly like talk shows.

## Key Principles

1. There is a stark difference between contributing (chicken) and being committed and accountable (pig)
2. Single-threaded leaders with genuine authority and accountability drive faster, better outcomes
3. Consensus-seeking often masks indecision, not collaboration
4. Assigning STLs forces strategic prioritization of initiatives
5. Action should be the default; meetings exist to solve problems, not report status

## Practical Takeaways

- **Establish your "pig" language** -- whether "STL," "DRI," "outcome owner," or "driver vs. passenger," create shared vocabulary that makes it safe to ask: "Who is the pig for this initiative?"
- **Appoint true STLs** with authority, accountability, time, clarity, and coaching; label decisions in documentation with the name of the responsible person
- **Spotlight ownership in action** through rituals recognizing individuals who take full ownership, sharing stories of their impact
- **Rethink meetings**: name the single accountable individual before discussion begins; aggressively cut meetings with weak agendas or dedicated solely to status updates; calculate the true cost of recurring meetings (hourly salary x attendees x duration x frequency) and target 25-50% reduction
- **Surface "chicken" behavior** with non-confrontational prompts: "Are you here to advise, or are you here to own?" and "Are you contributing expertise, or are you accountable for making this happen?"
- **Hire and assess for ownership** -- ask candidates: "What kind of problems do you like to own?" In performance reviews, evaluate: "Tell me about an instance where you saw a gap and filled it without being asked"
- **Implement a "chicken tax"** -- if someone attends a meeting without taking ownership, they contribute to a team fund (humorous, psychologically safe signal that passive participation is not acceptable)
- **Train on ownership** through "ownership incubators" -- small, low-risk projects where teams practice owning decisions with experienced mentors who guide then step back
