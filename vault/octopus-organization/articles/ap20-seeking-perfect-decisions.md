## The Antipattern

Organizations overrely on data, exhaustive analysis, and many stakeholders to remove as much uncertainty and risk as possible before making the "right" decision. The results are analysis paralysis, slow and mediocre decisions driven by consensus, and convoluted or diluted ownership of decision-making. Le-Brun and Werner root this in neuroscience: losses make us feel twice as bad as wins make us feel good, and our brains evolved to create certainty by finding predictable connections to survive, making most of us deeply uncomfortable with uncertainty.

## Why It Happens

- **Fear of failure**: Making no decision feels safer than making the wrong one, even when inaction itself becomes the failure
- **Cognitive biases**: Confirmation bias (favoring information that confirms beliefs), loss aversion (fear of losing out on unchosen options), sunk cost fallacy (harder to change minds after prolonged deliberation), and status quo bias (defaulting to inaction)
- **Choice overload**: The famous jam study showed that 24 options attracted more shoppers but resulted in only 3% sales vs. 30% at the six-option table -- ten times the sales from one-fourth the options
- **"Resulting"**: Annie Duke (former professional poker player and decision expert) identifies the trap of conflating decision quality with outcome. Good decisions can lead to bad results and vice versa; but when prolonged processes produce good outcomes, organizations credit the painful process as "sound" and set it as the bar
- **Political cover**: More people get pulled into decisions for political cover or perceived need for expertise, diluting accountability and slowing things down

Intel's indecision on mobile and GPU markets, fueled by data gathering and shareholder concerns, helped AMD gain ground and cost Intel billions. Even Octopus Organizations are not immune: Netflix's prolonged deliberation over an ad-supported tier saw competitors capture market share.

## The Octopus Way

**Distinguish high-stakes from low-stakes decisions.** High-stakes decisions are hard to reverse and carry significant negative consequences (e.g., picking a new CEO, opening a new location). Low-stakes decisions are reversible with a smaller "blast radius" (e.g., a temporary product discount, adding a website feature). Amazon uses the metaphor of one-way doors (high-stakes) and two-way doors (low-stakes), encouraging people to take two-way-door decisions with about 70% of the information they wish they had. Even for high-stakes decisions, Octopus Organizations maintain momentum with clear timelines, decision owners, and defined criteria.

**Fight decision paralysis with estimation (Fermi problems).** Nobel laureate Enrico Fermi's method breaks complex decisions into estimable parts based on reasonable assumptions. The authors illustrate with "How many piano tuners in Chicago?" -- connecting population, household, piano ownership, tuning frequency, and tuner capacity estimates to arrive at approximately 120 tuners. This approach:
- Breaks down intimidation
- Embraces uncertainty by accepting imperfect data
- Reveals hidden knowledge
- Focuses on critical variables
- Fosters probabilistic thinking
- Enables faster action

**View decisions as bets and adopt a probabilistic mindset.** "I'm not sure" or "I'm 70 percent certain" is not weakness but a vital step to seeing the world accurately. A Q1 sales estimate shifts from a single paralyzing number to a probable range you can manage.

**Shift from "picking" to "sorting."** Annie Duke's distinction: "picking" is the agonizing process of finding the single best option among several good ones; "sorting" is rapidly categorizing options into "good enough to try" and "not worth pursuing right now." When options are roughly equivalent and reversal cost is low, the goal is not to be perfectly right up front but to learn as fast as possible.

**Define tenets to speed up decision-making.** Simple, memorable principles that help teams quickly evaluate options: "Bias for action over endless analysis."

**Tightly manage the number of voices.** Smaller groups (ideally under five) are effective for high-velocity decisions, bringing diverse views, focusing on accuracy over confirmation, and rewarding truth-seeking.

**Channel disagreement productively with "disagree and commit."** All input is heard and valued, but once a decision is made, everyone commits. This requires trust that the decision-maker will listen to countervailing evidence and change course if proven wrong.

**Make decision-making a practice.** Document decisions along with assumptions, process, and pertinent factors for "fielding" -- the disciplined process of analyzing how much of an outcome was due to skill vs. luck. Ray Dalio's Bridgewater Associates uses a "believability factor," weighing input from those with a documented track record of good decisions.

## Key Principles

1. Most decisions are reversible -- treat them accordingly
2. 70% information is enough for low-stakes decisions
3. "Resulting" (conflating decision quality with outcome) creates vicious cycles of over-analysis
4. Sorting is faster and usually equivalent to picking when options are roughly similar
5. Decision-making improves through practice, documentation, and fielding

## Practical Takeaways

- **Accelerate decision speed** by asking: "How can I make this decision lower-stakes and faster?" and "What is the cost of delay versus the cost of being wrong?"
- **Review decisions as investments** -- record rationale, assumptions, strength of opinions, ranges for estimates, and dissenting views; review when results come in
- **Think in bets** -- express estimates and recommendations probabilistically; examine why beliefs might not be true
- **Practice "disagree and commit"** with structured dissent and devil's advocate roles
- **Check decision commitment** post-decision -- check in with dissenters who can safely provide new feedback; publicly reward dissenting views that improved final decisions
- **Define sort criteria up front** -- three to five simple must-haves to rapidly categorize options into "Meets the Bar" and "Doesn't"
- **Implement a "two good options" rule** -- for low-stakes reversible decisions, stop analysis as soon as two viable options exist and pick one quickly
