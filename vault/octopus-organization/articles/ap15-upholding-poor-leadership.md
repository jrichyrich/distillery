## The Antipattern

Organizations elevate, support, and leave in place leaders who undermine team potential and sow disengagement, often through power and dictate. The results are distrust, fear, disengagement, turnover, and lost productivity. Le-Brun and Werner frame this not primarily as a "bad people" problem but as an organizational behavior problem: the system tolerates and even rewards leadership that casts destructive shadows. Leaders can change, but lasting improvement only comes when organizations change the conditions that allow poor leadership to thrive.

## Why It Happens

Poor leadership emerges from multiple sources, many unintentional:

- **Impostor syndrome**: The pressure to be an expert with all the answers drives defensive, command-and-control behavior
- **Ingrained habits**: Being rewarded in the past for "being in charge" rather than "taking care of those in your charge"
- **Lack of self-awareness or empathy**: Never having operated in a collaborative mode, and no curiosity to develop these skills
- **Focus on trappings**: Leaders focus on status and control rather than enabling others
- **Blind spots**: Many leaders genuinely do not see how they come across; when pointed out constructively, they are happy to fix the behaviors

The authors describe several archetypes of toxic leadership (Table 15-1) and share vivid real-world examples:
- A Fortune 100 CEO ("William") who entered a meeting room and snapped at a new employee, "You're in my chair!" -- creating immediate distrust
- A UK bank CHRO who vacationed while her team executed massive personnel cuts, then returned and casually asked how it went
- A senior partner at a multinational professional services firm who sent a lecturing email (copied to many managers) berating someone for daring to contact him directly by email

The "shadow" metaphor is central: teams naturally align with their leader's priorities and mirror their behaviors. A leader who acts with impatience, dismissiveness, or avoidance fosters teams that do the same. Adam Blitzer, COO of Datadog, calls it the "blast radius" of poor leadership, contributing to an estimated $438 billion annual cost of lost productivity due to disengagement globally.

## The Octopus Way

**Redefining what leadership is:** Drawing on Professor Linda Hill of Harvard (who worked with Pixar's Ed Catmull), Octopus Organizations distinguish myths from realities of leadership (Table 15-2). Leadership shifts from a role defined by position and authority to a behavior defined by trust, capability, and influence that transcends formal titles.

**Openly sharing leadership style and inviting feedback:** High-energy leaders do not suppress their energy but develop self-awareness to manage its impact. The fast-talking leader learns to pause, listen actively, and ensure others feel heard -- adapting delivery without changing core personality.

**Weeding out shadow-casting behaviors in hiring and development:**
- Screen for empathy and capacity to hear others
- Use interview questions like: "What's the most interesting piece of feedback you've received recently?" and "What's something you've changed about how you work in the past year?"
- Revise job descriptions and promotion criteria to explicitly include social skills beyond "nice-to-haves"
- Invest in coaching targeting active listening, empathetic communication, conflict resolution, and cross-cultural collaboration

**Democratizing leadership:** When leadership is treated as a behavior anyone can practice, it emerges organically. People without formal authority step up to guide, solve, or mentor. Team members gravitate toward colleagues who embody emotional intelligence and empathy.

## Key Principles

1. Leadership is a behavior, not a role -- it emerges from trust, capability, and influence
2. The root problem is organizational tolerance of poor leadership, not individual "bad leaders"
3. Self-awareness is the starting point for transformation
4. Empathy, humility, and curiosity are the antithesis of shadow-casting behaviors
5. Leaders should aim to "take care of those in their charge" rather than "be in charge"

## Practical Takeaways

- **Speak last** -- hold opinions until others have shared views, preventing premature convergence
- **Build a shadow cabinet** of trusted individuals with diverse perspectives who assess leadership styles and challenge assumptions
- **Periodically check five questions**: Do you clearly communicate a vision? Treat people with respect? Solicit contrary opinions? Encourage others' ideas? Truly listen in meetings?
- **Connect through shared experience** -- immerse in hands-on work alongside teams as a learner, not a director
- **Stop one thing** -- identify one specific behavior through feedback that casts a negative shadow (e.g., interrupting, dismissive tone, checking phone in meetings) and track progress on changing it
- **Cultivate self-awareness** through journaling: note situations where poor leadership behaviors crept in and reflect on more constructive responses
- **Model empathy and vulnerability** -- share struggles and learnings openly
