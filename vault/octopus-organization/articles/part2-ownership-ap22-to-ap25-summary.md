## Overview

Part 2 of *The Octopus Organization* by Phil Le-Brun and Jana Werner addresses how organizations increase ownership -- the sense of agency, accountability, and intrinsic drive that enables teams to solve problems autonomously. The four antipatterns in this section (#22-25) trace a logical arc from the front door to the foundations: who you hire, how you organize them, what motivates them, and whether you actually let them own anything.

The central thesis is that ownership cannot be declared -- it must be built through systems of trust, purpose, and enablement. Tin Man organizations profess ownership while undermining it at every step through poor hiring, star-performer worship, extrinsic incentive addiction, and micromanagement disguised as empowerment.

## The Four Antipatterns

### [[Antipattern #22: Hiring Poorly]]
Organizations hire transactionally for current skills rather than long-term potential, and treat onboarding as administrative overhead. Octopus Organizations use bar raisers (Amazon's model) to raise the hiring bar with every new hire, screen for learning agility and values alignment over credentials, and invest in comprehensive onboarding lasting up to a full year. The key insight is that hiring is the first and most consequential ownership decision an organization makes.

### [[Antipattern #23: Prizing Individuals over Teams]]
The "super chicken" problem: organizations collect brilliant jerks and wonder why collaboration fails. William Muir's landmark chicken study and the Shane Battier basketball analogy illustrate that the fundamental unit of value creation is the team, not the individual. Octopus Organizations hire for humble, hungry, and smart -- qualities that create "halo radius" instead of "blast radius." DBS Bank's evolution from functional silos to cross-functional squads with "mini-CEOs" provides the transformation blueprint. Long-lived teams serve as learning amplifiers, early signal detectors, and pattern-breaking forces.

### [[Antipattern #24: Mismanaging Incentives]]
Organizations conflate incentives (forward-looking carrots) with rewards (retrospective recognition), defaulting to financial incentives that feel coercive, spark destructive competition, narrow focus, and promote short-termism. Drawing on Herzberg's two-factor theory and Pink's *Drive*, Octopus Organizations harness intrinsic motivation through purpose, autonomy, and mastery. Real-world reward systems from Zappos (WoW Awards), Southwest Airlines (Heroes of the Heart), McDonald's (Circle of Excellence), Netflix (freedom and ownership), and Airbnb (Live and Work Anywhere) demonstrate that intrinsic motivators are harder for competitors to match than pay raises.

### [[Antipattern #25: Granting Artificial Ownership]]
The capstone antipattern: organizations believe they have distributed ownership when they have not. Ownership has two failure modes -- leaders who cannot give it (perfectionism, habit, fear, envy, lack of coaching skills) and employees who cannot take it (habit, unclear boundaries, risk/reward imbalance, organizational complexity). Octopus Organizations acknowledge artificial ownership exists, clearly define what ownership means (authority to make decisions with clear boundaries, not vague feeling of being "heard"), use principles and guardrails rather than procedures, and shift leaders from command to coach. Reed Hastings's aspiration to go entire quarters without making a decision and Patagonia's commander's intent represent the end state.

## Cross-Cutting Themes

**The Hiring-to-Ownership Pipeline.** The four antipatterns form a dependency chain: poor hiring (#22) produces brilliant jerks (#23) who thrive under extrinsic incentives (#24) in organizations that promise but do not deliver ownership (#25). Fixing only one link leaves the chain broken.

**Trust as Infrastructure.** Every antipattern reveals a trust deficit. Bar raisers build trust in hiring quality. Team-first culture builds trust between colleagues. Rewards over incentives build trust in organizational fairness. True ownership builds trust between leaders and teams. As Alicia Greenwood notes: "It takes a lot to build a culture of ownership but very little to destroy it."

**The Leader as Coach.** The recurring transformation is from command to coaching. Leaders stop giving answers and start asking questions: "What outcomes are you hoping for?" "What options have you already thought about?" "What support do you need?" Focus praise on effort, strategy, and learning rather than innate talent.

**Teams as the Unit of Work.** From DBS Bank's "Managing through Journeys" to Nucor's team-based bonuses to the Navy SEALs' after-action reviews, the consistent message is that organizing around teams -- not functions or individuals -- is the structural prerequisite for genuine ownership.
