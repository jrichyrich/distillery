# The Octopus Organization: A Better Way to Operate

## Central Thesis

Phil Le-Brun and Jana Werner argue that most organizations today operate like the **Tin Man** from *The Wizard of Oz*: rigid, clumsy, slow to move and react, lacking heart, struggling with empathy, capable of taking instructions but showing little initiative. His tool of choice is a blunt axe. When Dorothy found him, he had completely rusted up. These organizations are "beautifully engineered for a world that no longer exists. They're not bad; they're just ... inadequate."

The alternative is the **Octopus Organization** -- modeled on the remarkable adaptability, distributed intelligence, fluid movement, and innate curiosity of the octopus. Two-thirds of an octopus's neurons are not in its brain but in its eight arms, which can think and act for themselves while also coordinating with the brain as a team. Octopus Organizations similarly tap the intelligence of people and teams distributed throughout the organization, sensing subtle signals, learning, and adapting instantly to navigate uncertainty.

## Why the Tin Man Model Persists

The Tin Man organizational model is, in essence, still a factory. For decades, standardization, specialization, control, measuring individual performance, compliance, mass production, and predictable outputs achieved immeasurably great results. But it is now "a rusting construct -- more than a century old."

Tin Man organizations are built on a foundation of **permission**: permission to innovate, permission to speak up, permission to even care. They are designed to minimize risk, eliminate variation, and create efficient, predictable outcomes. They cannot get beyond average because they are not structured to allow it.

## The Transformation Trap

Despite trillions invested in transformations over the past two decades, the results are underwhelming:

- Less than 12% of transformations create expected sustainable performance gains 36 months in
- Less than a third of organizations realize anticipated revenue benefits; just a quarter achieve cost benefits
- 70-90% of digital transformation initiatives end in failure

Transformations fail because organizations use a Tin Man approach to their transformation -- rigid, linear, top-down, measured with simple metrics. They treat the organization as a machine to be fixed through targeted interventions rather than a complex, living system. "True change doesn't come from artifacts -- it comes from how people see the world and from changing behavior, not structures."

The cruel irony: to escape their Tin Man roots, organizations engage in a transformation that represents the apotheosis of Tin Man behavior.

## Complicated vs. Complex

A fundamental distinction drives the book:

| Complicated (Tin Man) | Complex (Octopus) |
|---|---|
| Predictable cause-and-effect | Emergence -- outcomes from interactions that are not linear or predictable |
| Broken into smaller components, managed through centralized control | Managed through learning, resilience, and collaboration |
| Defined blueprints, assembled linearly | Adaptability, decentralized ownership, interconnectedness |
| Short-term focus: quarterly targets, simple metrics, risk minimization | Long-term vision: thinking big, responsible risk-taking |
| Run *or* change the business | Run *and* change the business as a single continuous dynamic |

"If you take the welder out of the boatmaking factory, you can guess what happens. Try to understand, then explain, what happens if you grab one arm of an octopus."

## The Three Pillars: Clarity, Ownership, Curiosity

The 36 antipatterns are organized into three categories that form a **virtuous cycle**:

1. **Creating Clarity** -- Shared understanding of purpose, values, priorities, and what success looks like. Cuts through bureaucratic fog so people understand which problems are worth solving and can confidently make decisions. "Clarity is cocreated, not just delivered by fiat."

2. **Increasing Ownership** -- Responsibility plus authorship. "When people own their work, when they feel responsible for an outcome, not just a task, magic happens. They bring their whole selves. They problem-solve. They care." Ownership is not delegated; it is ignited.

3. **Inciting Curiosity** -- A continuous current of adaptation and bold reinvention, a relentless humble posture of learning over knowing, questioning sacred assumptions, and asking "what if" about everything taken for granted. Where "failure is feedback and leaders are positively discontented with the status quo."

The cycle: Clarity enables ownership (people understand what problems to solve). Ownership creates curiosity (let me own a challenge and I will experiment and learn my way through it). Curiosity increases clarity (learning swings around to improve shared understanding), which further increases ownership.

## How to Use the Book

The book contains 36 antipatterns -- "conditioned, formulaic responses to complex challenges that, despite good intentions and surface-level appeal, consistently make things worse." Each entry identifies the Tin Man behavior, describes what Octopus Organizations do instead, and provides concrete "levers" to pull -- points of intervention where a small shift can produce disproportionately large changes throughout the entire system.

The book is designed to be used modularly, not read straight through. There is no recipe or playbook for a one-time Octopus transformation. "This is about patterns, not plans. There's no date when you'll say, 'We're done. Now we're an Octopus.'" The concluding chapter provides a compass, not a map.

## Key Contrasts: Tin Man vs. Octopus in Practice

- **Strategy meetings**: Tin Man uses preset agendas, carefully prepared slides, controlled information flow. Octopus gathers diverse groups around a whiteboard, encourages provocative questions, builds on and challenges ideas, explores tangents -- "the energy is palpable."
- **Call centers**: Tin Man reps stick to scripts and decision trees, rewarded for volume. Octopus agents own the customer's problem, actively listen, empathize, and tailor solutions -- perhaps with a discretionary budget.
- **Innovation**: Tin Man segregates innovation in labs isolated from the core business. Octopus makes innovation a responsibility of everyone, embedded in daily work, with ideas encouraged from anywhere.

Customer-centric companies can be more than three times as likely to lead their industries in revenue growth. Amazon Prime did not start as a gigantic strategic bet -- it began as one person's idea that developed into massive customer value through the collective intelligence of the organization.
