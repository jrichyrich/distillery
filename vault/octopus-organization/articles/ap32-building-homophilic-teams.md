# Antipattern #32: Building Homophilic Teams

## The Antipattern

Tin Man organizations form teams of people with similar mindsets and backgrounds to create cohesion and harmony, and for perceived progress. "Culture fit" becomes a justification for hiring people who think in similar ways, valuing the perceived efficiency of implicit understanding -- working with someone who "gets it" immediately. But this apparent efficiency masks a dangerous reality: groupthink, blind spots, and ultimately worse decisions.

A revealing study showed that groups joined by an outsider felt less effective and less confident in their decisions -- but the same study showed that adding an outsider **doubled** their chances of arriving at the correct solution. The work feels harder, but outcomes are significantly better.

Le-Brun and Werner are precise about what "diversity" means here: not just demographic diversity, but **cognitive and intellectual diversity** -- different problem-solving approaches, decision-making styles, skills, knowledge, experience, working and thinking styles, and preferences for how individuals gather, process, and assimilate data.

**Tin Man results:**
- Poor decision-making due to blind spots
- Reduced team adaptability and resilience
- Stifled innovation and curiosity

## Why It Happens

- **Industrial legacy**: Factory models prized finding people who fit seamlessly into existing processes; having a different perspective was heretical
- **Comfort over adaptability**: The desire for comfort and control overrides the necessity for adaptability and innovation
- **Fluency heuristic**: When information feels difficult to process or unfamiliar, we tend to assume it's wrong (compared to easier-to-understand data)
- **Efficiency illusion**: Like-minded teams feel more efficient but consider fewer options and are less likely to identify blind spots, yet paradoxically carry higher confidence in their views
- **Confirmation bias**: The absence of conflict is mistaken for productive harmony
- **Board-level pattern**: Homophilic relationships on corporate boards and between CEOs and directors have been linked to lower levels of monitoring and scrutiny, reducing organizational performance

## The Octopus Way

Octopus Organizations understand that cognitive diversity is key for problem-solving, decision-making, and innovation. Harvard professor Linda Hill encourages what she terms **creative abrasion** -- built on three elements:

1. **Intellectual diversity** and the feeling of safety to raise contrarian views
2. **A marketplace of ideas** -- forums where ideas are debated, mixed, and shared
3. **Cognitive conflict** with clear rules of engagement to improve and elevate ideas without lapsing into interpersonal conflict

**Key Octopus behaviors:**

- **Generate ideas independently first.** People generate more and better ideas independently before coming together as a group. At Warby Parker, employees spend a few minutes weekly writing down innovation ideas that colleagues read and comment on, yielding about 400 new ideas quarterly.
- **Assemble cross-functional critique groups.** Pixar created "dailies" where work was brought before a diverse team for critique, creating proximity for diverse thinkers.
- **Positively amplify cognitive conflict.** Good leaders understand that constructive cognitive conflict drives better decisions. Pixar trained people to advocate for their point of view and critically evaluate options, using ground rules: "Assume positive intent," "Attack the idea, not the person," "Listen to understand, not just to respond."
- **Introduce constraints.** Deadlines and budgets (like Pixar's unyielding film release dates) force teams to evaluate key assumptions and sharpen thinking.
- **Avoid weak compromises.** Press for better, more integrated alternatives rather than accepting suboptimal solutions to maintain social cohesion.
- **Reimagine hiring.** Don't put a high premium on "fitting in"; identify candidates who bring new perspectives while aligning with organizational values. Use structured interviews and bias-mitigation techniques.
- **Monitor for like-mindedness.** Watch for indicators: Are new hires consistently rejected from or leaving a team? Is dissent heard? Is there diversity of leadership strengths? Is dwell time being tracked?

Diverse teams are more likely to: raise facts in debates, correct errors during deliberations, reexamine facts and remain objective, make decisions twice as quickly with half the meetings, and make better decisions 87 percent of the time.

## Key Principles

1. **Cognitive diversity over demographic diversity** -- the focus is on different ways of thinking, not just different backgrounds (though the two often correlate)
2. **Harder is better** -- diverse teams feel more exhausting, but this constructive friction is what drives superior outcomes
3. **Creative abrasion requires structure** -- clear norms, rules of engagement, and conflict-resolution steps make cognitive conflict productive rather than divisive
4. **Culture-add, not culture-fit** -- hiring should seek people who bring new perspectives, not people who mirror existing team members

## Real-World Examples

- **Google Glass**: Team of engineers and technologists with similar vantage points created an echo chamber; they explained away external feedback signaling problems, leading to one of tech's most famous product failures
- **Warby Parker**: Weekly written innovation ideas from employees yield ~400 new ideas quarterly
- **Pixar**: "Dailies" and structured debates with ground rules; unyielding deadlines as constraints for creative abrasion
- **Amazon**: HR evangelist Stephen Brozovich advises monitoring team dynamics for signs of growing like-mindedness

## Practical Takeaways

1. **Establish clear norms for creative abrasion** -- cocreate principles like "Challenge ideas, not people" and document them in a charter
2. **Anchor conflict in shared purpose** -- reference collective intent during decision-making to prevent disagreements from becoming personal
3. **Institutionalize constructive dissent** -- use Oxford-style debates (argue both for and against) and fishbowl meetings (inner circle debates, outer circle observes)
4. **Invest in conflict-management development** -- formally train people in inclusive communication and conflict management
5. **Diversify idea generation** -- use individual written submissions before group discussions or anonymous feedback platforms
6. **Encourage cross-pollination** -- mandate time with customers, different departments, or cross-functional projects
7. **Use cognitive empathy programs** -- workshops, storytelling sessions, and personality assessments to foster genuine curiosity for diverse cognitive styles
