## The Antipattern

Tin Man organizations treat all failures as equal, as inefficient, a threat to short-term goals (such as stock price), and therefore an outcome to be avoided or penalized. The results are a risk-averse culture, suppression of experimentation, creative problem-solving, and learning, and bigger failures from avoiding small, controlled experiments.

## Why It Happens

While 91 percent of C-level executives claim they encourage experimentation, only 35 percent of ground-level teams report this translating into reality. Most organizations are terrified of failure -- it is treated as expensive, embarrassing, and competitively disadvantageous. Leaders worry that tolerating failure invites people to give less than their best.

Tin Man organizations thrive on knowing the "right" answer and making safe choices. The relentless pressure to deliver predictable, positive quarterly results discounts curiosity. Nobody wants to explain a performance dip because they tried something new. One Fortune 500 CIO told the authors he cannot fail with new technology introduction because culture and power politics would exploit the failure -- yet his R&D unit is expected to experiment and fail daily.

Hierarchy compounds the problem: middle management becomes a bottleneck, caught between executive demands for innovation and incentives focused on hitting numbers. On a personal level, failure feels painful and shameful, and often leads to career repercussions. Cognitive traps reinforce this: prospect theory (losing feels worse than winning feels good), confirmation bias (we like ideas we agree with), and fundamental attribution error (our failures are not our fault but others' failures are theirs).

Organizations find creative ways to evade failure without realizing it. "Non-experiments" are engineered to succeed: one company tested a new fried chicken sandwich in a region known for loving fried chicken sandwiches. The test was a smashing success. The national launch flopped. Evading failure also amplifies sunk costs -- doomed initiatives are propped up when the company should follow the first rule of holes: when you are in one, stop digging.

## The Octopus Way

**Failure as Necessary Learning.** Octopus Organizations view failure as an inevitable consequence of being curious and learning -- but do not conflate this with "fail fast, break things," which almost sets failing as a goal. As Ray Dalio puts it: "Fail well and love your failures and see them as opportunity to improve." Astro Teller illustrates learning's value by asking people how long it would take to recreate what they built if they lost everything -- on average, 10 percent of the original time. The other 90 percent was critical learning. He calls these "moonshot compost" on which new projects grow.

**Not All Failures Are Equal.** Octopus Organizations distinguish between "good failure" (learning through intelligent experimentation), "complex failures" (familiar systems breaking due to unexpected event combinations), and "preventable failures" (deviations from known processes due to skill gaps). They analyze problematic failures beyond superficial first-order answers using Amy Edmondson's "thoughtful reflection" and cross-functional root-cause analysis. WD-40 CEO Garry Ridge reframes these as learning moments with "a positive or negative outcome that needs to be openly and freely shared to benefit all."

**Normalizing Failure.** Octopus leaders position potential failure as a source of valuable data. One executive at a failing insurance company placed a large jar of glass marbles on the table during a meeting -- each marble representing a customer lost every day. The jar took away the pressure of avoiding experimental failure by making existing complex failure visible and tangible. Ferrari CEO Benedetto Vigna publicly talks about failures in investor relations calls and press to signal that failure is natural and expected.

**Depersonalizing Failure through Hypotheses.** Leaders change language from execution to discovery. They frame work as hypotheses to be tested, creating psychological distance between individuals and their ideas. "We are testing if this should be built" rather than "We are building this." If the hypothesis is disproven, the experiment still succeeds because it generated valuable knowledge.

**Lowering the Cost of Failure.** Organizations run many small, inexpensive experiments. By keeping failure costs low, they remove the fear that paralyzes decision-making. Ironically, avoiding small controlled experiments amplifies exposure to catastrophic failures. "You don't explore with a perfect map. You need to send out scouts."

**Premortems.** Teams imagine their project has failed spectacularly and brainstorm what went wrong. Astro told his Wing team: "It's three months from now. We had our launch one month ago, and it was so bad, we feel physically sick. Write down why we're gonna screw up so badly." The team furiously documented everything that could go wrong, then worked intensively for two months -- and the launch went flawlessly.

**Letting Go Gracefully.** Octopus Organizations learn in a "passionately dispassionate" way -- passionate about innovation and customers while remaining disciplined critics of their own work. Many create "quit criteria" that tell them when to stop. Annie Duke, drawing from professional poker, says every bet should include clear criteria for when to fold.

## Key Principles

- Octopus Organizations do not fail fast; they learn quickly and efficiently
- The best way to avoid catastrophic failure is to embrace small, controlled, intelligent failures
- Rewarding intelligent failure counteracts the natural inclination to hide failures to protect reputations, bonuses, or promotions
- Prioritize learning over features: create an Important List and a Learning List; the Learning List changes the Important List
- Start with hypotheses: "We believe that X change will lead to Y improvement for Z reason"
- Reframe success metrics from outcomes to learning velocity -- measure how quickly hypotheses are proven or disproven

## Real-World Examples

- **Alphabet X / Astro Teller**: "Moonshot compost," premortems, standing ovations for public sharing of failures
- **Ray Dalio**: "Fail well and love your failures"
- **WD-40 (Garry Ridge)**: Reframes failures as learning moments to be openly shared
- **Ferrari (Benedetto Vigna)**: Publicly discusses failures in investor calls and with press
- **Annie Duke**: Quit criteria -- every bet should include clear criteria for when to fold
- **Datadog (Adam Blitzer)**: "Debrief, celebrate it, why was it valuable, how will it shape the next two things we will do?"
- **Insurance company executive**: Jar of glass marbles representing daily customer losses made existing failure visible and reduced pressure around experimental failure
- **Fried chicken sandwich test**: Non-experiment tested in a region guaranteed to love the product -- national launch flopped

## Practical Takeaways

1. Create a "failure resume" -- a public list of worst failures, a cathartic exercise that challenges others to consider whether they are taking enough risks
2. Celebrate the courage to fail: give standing ovations, bonuses, and prime spots in new initiatives to those who proved something does not work
3. Practice blameless postmortems using the "5 Whys" technique to uncover deeper second- and third-order reasons for failures
4. Make current failures visible: place a visible reminder in a prominent place showing the real cost of the current situation
5. Establish quit criteria before starting projects so stopping is seen as good stewardship
6. Prioritize learning over features using the Important List / Learning List technique
