## The Antipattern

Le-Brun and Werner describe top leadership operating as a collection of specialized experts more beholden to their individual functions than to the enterprise's success. The results are conflicting agendas and turf wars that create organizational drag, an inward focus that undermines customer value, and hampered decision-making.

The authors illustrate with a common enterprise goal of "becoming more customer-centric" that splinters across functions:
- Finance: "We'll cut costs to keep customers' prices down."
- Operations: "We'll open more stores to meet customers where they are."
- Engineering: "We'll make new products that address customer needs."
- Marketing: "We'll reduce the number of products to reduce customer confusion."

These approaches are irreconcilable without genuine team alignment, leading to battles between finance and operations over costs, and engineering and marketing over product lines.

## Why It Happens

Le-Brun and Werner identify several structural and psychological drivers:

- **Individual accountability spotlight**: C-suite roles carry intense accountability for domain-specific results; success there gets celebrated and compensated.
- **Personal summit protection**: Senior roles represent a professional pinnacle; leaders have an understandable but often unstated inclination to safeguard hard-won authority and distinct identity.
- **Increasing specialization**: The cognitive load of mastering complex functional areas means executives arrive with more specialization and less understanding of other functions than ever.
- **Resource competition**: Traditional organizational dynamics pit functions against each other in subtle competition for resources.
- **Divergent time horizons**: A CFO thinks in quarters; an innovation officer thinks in years.

One study found that while leadership teams agree aligning on purpose is critical, only 60% report being actually aligned. The authors note that "mostly aligned" is insufficient -- "just as 'mostly aligned' wheels on a car aren't sufficient."

## The Octopus Way

Octopus Organizations cultivate a true leadership team whose primary allegiance is to the customer and organization, transcending departmental loyalties.

- **"First team" mentality** (Patrick Lencioni's concept): Each leader's overriding responsibility is to the collective success of the organization. "My success is inextricably linked to our success."
- **Anchor in shared purpose and strategy**: Deep dialogue ensures every leader can articulate and champion core purpose, strategy, and key priorities. Written definitions of terms like "customer-centric" and "controlled growth" create shared understanding.
- **Develop shared mental models through robust debate**: Throwaway phrases like "let's become digital" are met with scrutiny. Leaders challenge each other to surface assumptions on the spot. The authors note that fewer than one-fifth of leaders understand what "digital" or "agentic AI" means for their business.
- **Build "relational glue"**: David Risher (CEO, Lyft) starts every executive team meeting with "How are we as a team?" -- establishing that team health matters alongside business results.
- **Create shared clarity rituals**: Dame Julia Hoggett (CEO, London Stock Exchange) holds 9 a.m. Monday meetings with up to thirty leaders to discuss priorities and hurdles, calling on those facing the biggest issues first, with new joiners going last to observe the culture.
- **Design for interdependence**: Strategic initiatives are co-led by executives from different functions. A significant portion of leadership bonuses are tied to overall company performance. The authors observed that implementing joint incentives at a UK retail bank "drastically changed their openness for collaborating."
- **Continuously revalidate alignment**: At Gallo Winery, CIO Robert Barrios talks about "revalidating what winning looks like" with the executive team. Dynafit CEO Benedikt Bohm regularly revisits the "lagebild" (situation picture) with his leadership team.

## Key Principles

1. The executive group is each leader's "first team" -- enterprise success before functional success
2. Shared understanding of strategy requires ongoing, deep dialogue, not one-time exercises
3. Psychological safety must be deliberately established at the board and executive level
4. Alignment drifts naturally and requires constant maintenance
5. Joint incentives and shared accountability change behavior more than exhortation

## Real-World Examples

- **Lyft (David Risher)**: Starts every executive team meeting with "How are we as a team?" to build relational glue and team health.
- **London Stock Exchange (Dame Julia Hoggett)**: Monday 9 a.m. leadership meetings with up to thirty leaders create unity of purpose and shared clarity.
- **UK retail bank digital transformation**: Joint incentives for achieving goals drastically changed leadership openness for collaboration.
- **Gallo Winery (Robert Barrios)**: "Revalidating what winning looks like" within the executive team and cascading alignment to individual contributors.
- **Dynafit (Benedikt Bohm)**: CEO regularly revisits the "lagebild" (situation picture) with the leadership team.
- **Amazon**: SVP roles tied to customer outcomes ("stores," "devices and services") rather than abstract internal functions -- no chief data, digital, transformation, or information officer.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Test for alignment**: Have leadership team members independently write what a goal or strategy means and what success looks like; compare answers to spot dissonance.
- **Read back meetings**: At the end of each meeting, have a randomly selected leader share three key takeaways.
- **Link roles to value**: Ensure leadership titles relate to customer or business outcomes rather than abstract internal skills.
- **Join up incentives and metrics**: Propose that the leadership team collectively track and own one specific, measurable outcome requiring joint effort for the upcoming quarter.
- **Declare your "first team" allegiance**: Explicitly communicate that the senior leadership group is your primary team.
- **Cocreate and own strategic priorities**: Facilitate leadership sessions to debate, refine, and collectively co-own the top three to five priorities; stress-test quarterly.
- **Hold "silo-busting" problem-solving sessions**: Dedicate a quarterly leadership meeting to tackling one significant problem exacerbated by siloed thinking.
- **Whiteboard your top three shared battles**: Identify the top three critical "battles" the organization must win in six to twelve months.
