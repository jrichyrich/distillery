# Conclusion: Becoming the Octopus

After thirty-six antipatterns describing what's broken, Le-Brun and Werner address the three questions leaders immediately ask: How do I get started? How do I make sure it sticks? How do I scale it? Their answer reframes the third question entirely.

## Three Guiding Principles

The authors urge leaders to resist reaching for the transformation playbook -- benchmarking, analysis, grand planning, armies of consultants, top-down dictates are all Tin Man behavior. Instead, approach the journey with three principles:

### 1. Do Change With People, Not To Them

Tin Man organizations "put the power of interpretation in the hands of a few." Their leaders believe their job is to design a perfect transformation plan and push it down. The Octopus leader understands their true leverage comes from acting as an **architect of the environment, not the architect of the plan**. They obsess over creating conditions -- clarity of purpose, psychological safety, and access to information -- that allow emergent solutions to flourish from the people closest to the work. Senior leaders rarely drive change; they cultivate the organization's innate capacity to change itself.

### 2. Entwine Learning and Impact

Tin Man organizations mistake activity for progress. Octopus Organizations know that progress is activity generating both value and learning. They embed experiments into the daily flow of work. "Cutting meeting time in half is just activity; learning how to have more effective meetings and making better decisions as a result is progress." Not every experiment will yield expected value, but it must yield deep learning to inform the next set of experiments.

### 3. Do Less to Achieve More

The Tin Man's default is to add: a process, a tool, a new layer of oversight. The Octopus leader operates with a **subtractive mindset** -- true leverage often comes from removing what is in the way. As AWS's Jake Burns notes, "Work expands to fill the time available for its completion." Starting small with a single team generates frequent, visible progress far more powerful for building long-term engagement than a single massive "win."

These mantras shift organizations from periodic large-scale scarring transformations to a state where people close to the action take ownership. The shift won't happen everywhere at once -- there will be Octopus-ish parts coexisting with old Tin Men. Where new behaviors take hold, a **flywheel effect** sets in (per Jim Collins), underpinned by positive dissatisfaction with the status quo.

## Understanding the Levers

Drawing from Donella Meadows's systems thinking research on leverage points, the authors categorize their levers into three strengths:

### Small Levers
Adjust parameters in existing processes -- quick to apply but limited power. Example: reducing approvers from eleven to two. Meadows notes that "probably 99 percent of our attention goes to [small levers], but there's not a lot of leverage in them."

### Mid-Level Levers
Reshape system structure, information flows, and feedback loops:
- **Restructure material and information flows** -- reducing handoffs from 160 to 20 isn't just process improvement; it's fundamental restructuring
- **Strengthen balancing feedback loops** -- like a thermostat: customer complaints spike, trigger investigation, fixes reduce future errors
- **Amplify reinforcing feedback loops** -- engines of growth where success breeds success (e.g., employee recognition creating virtuous cycles)

### Profound Levers
The most powerful interventions, in order of increasing impact:
- **Rules of the system** -- changing from bureaucratic approval to principles-based decision-making
- **Self-organization** -- enhancing the system's innate ability to evolve and create its own order through clear goals, rapid feedback, and distributed ownership
- **Goals** -- shifting the primary success metric (e.g., from "quarterly profit" to "customer lifetime value") changes the trajectory of the entire system
- **Mental models** -- the most powerful leverage point of all. A paradigm shift can happen in a millisecond in an individual's mind, requiring no capital investment. Octopus leaders create dissonance by pointing out failures of the old way, showcasing undeniable successes from the new paradigm, and amplifying voices of change agents

Jeff Wilkie, former Amazon CEO of Worldwide Consumer, put Meadows's model into practice as a taxonomy of levers at Amazon.

## Start Small: Change One Antipattern

The practical approach:

1. **Identify the antipattern.** Look for emotional reactions -- aggressive nodding, nervous laughter, frowning, rolling eyes. Sources include new employees questioning norms, employee survey frustrations, exit interviews, help desk tickets. Resist forcing ownership on people; seek who is most passionate. Ironically, asking those most strongly opposed to own the change can work -- if the change works, critics become staunchest advocates.

2. **Develop a hypothesis.** Define which levers to pull. Make clear that proving the hypothesis wrong is just as valuable as proving it right. Don't overengineer; set them, test them, uncover insights.

3. **Run an experiment and reflect.** Experiments can deprecate a frustrating practice (stopping something!), deviate from established processes temporarily, or design something new. Include a "fire extinguisher" -- budget for unexpected costs, a backup team, and a "we're trying something here" memo.

4. **Practice double-loop learning.** Beyond the single-loop (what happened? what actions to take?), go deeper: Why did we choose this experiment? What underlying assumptions led us here? This challenges existing mental models and identifies deeper systemic issues.

The **Antipattern Escape Canvas** structures this process: single-threaded owner, time box, Tin Man diagnosis, hypothesis, experiment design, resources needed, success indicators, learning capture, and next steps.

## Spread, Don't Scale

When experiments succeed, leaders immediately ask "How do we scale this?" But scaling betrays Tin Man thinking -- it becomes shorthand for uniform implementation. As former Schneider Electric President Tanuja Randery insists: "The idea that things must be perfectly the same everywhere is nonsense."

Scaling risks:
- Turning experimental successes into broad failures
- Robbing people of local ownership
- Ignoring context by assuming the org is a machine with replaceable cogs
- Separating learning from doing
- Missing local value because "if it doesn't scale, we shouldn't support it"

Instead, Octopus Organizations **spread** -- ideas and practices flow organically from team to team based on need and context. A pull model reinforces ownership ("Do I want to take this?") and allows curiosity ("How can I adapt this?").

**Stephen Brozovich's story**: As a web developer twenty years ago, frustrated with arcane command-line image posting, he taught himself just enough programming to build a visual image search tool. No mandate, no promotion of the tool -- it was pulled through Amazon because it worked. He received Amazon's "Just Do It" award (a used oversized Nike shoe with an old sock, handed to him by Jeff Bezos).

Benefits of spreading over scaling: behaviors are more likely to stick, a culture of change becomes embedded, more diverse solutions create resilience, and positive tension between competing solutions fuels new thinking.

## When You Do Scale: Platforms and Mechanisms, Not Practices

Some things should be scaled -- but deliberately. Scale **foundational platforms** (shared secure cloud environments, common brand and safety principles) and **effective mechanisms** (robust hiring processes, shared frameworks for big bets). The goal: provide a strong foundation that accelerates and liberates decentralized teams.

Amazon uses **mechanisms** -- tools and processes that guide behavior (not mandate it) beyond direct leadership. They shift focus from outputs to controllable inputs. Each mechanism needs: a tool, an adoption strategy, and a process for inspection. Note: mechanisms don't force behavior change -- "Figure out what software will help you target ads at scale" rather than "Buy a software package we tell you to buy."

## Lead to Not Lead

Leaders struggle more than their teams with becoming Octopus-like. As Aaron Dignan notes: "You're trying to lead your organization to a place where you're not the leader anymore, at least not in the way you are today."

The leader's job: help people who have been managed all their lives to take ownership. Facilitate clarity, remove obstacles, celebrate curiosity. Common characteristics of Octopus leaders:

- **Lead with humility** -- see themselves as temporary caretakers; subjugate ego to bring out the best in others
- **Practice voracious curiosity** -- replace "commanding" with "questioning"; listen to fully understand, not to form a reply
- **Act as system architects** -- work on, not in, the system; focus on designing the environment and removing friction
- **Default to trust** -- start with the belief that people are capable and want to do good work
- **Provide stability** -- consistency comes from unwavering commitment to purpose, not rigid adherence to plans
- **Be a source of positive energy** -- genuinely believe in people's ability to solve hard problems; celebrate progress no matter how small

The book closes with the octopus metaphor: huge, inquisitive eyes looking into the distance while tentacles explore immediate surroundings -- changing color and reshaping based on what's learned. "The problems organizations face are known and the answers are within reach, if they just create clarity, provide ownership, and foster curiosity."
