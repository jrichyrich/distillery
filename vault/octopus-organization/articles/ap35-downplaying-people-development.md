# Antipattern #35: Downplaying People Development

## The Antipattern

Tin Man organizations neglect or minimize people development, treating it as separate from or secondary to "real work." They are impatient gardeners -- strewing seeds, adding last year's leftover fertilizer, watering when they think of it, then blaming the seeds for poor results rather than the cultivation process.

Nearly three-quarters of US companies resort to outside hires to acquire needed skills, because employee development is implicitly not seen as real work. Despite telling employees that "people are our most important asset," organizations spend $340 billion annually on corporate training of questionable efficacy while the average employee gets as little as 24 minutes weekly for learning. More than 56 percent of employees report their organizations don't encourage skill development to protect against technological disruption.

Le-Brun and Werner share personal examples: for Phil in the 1990s it was "Total Quality Management" imposed in an unready environment; for Jana it was "SAFe" in the 2010s -- trendy training brought in on executive whim rather than identified organizational needs.

**Tin Man results:**
- Critical skills gaps and shortages hinder productivity
- Disengaged workforce and reduced retention
- Reduced innovation leading to competitive vulnerability

## Why It Happens

- **Training as luxury expense**: In the old industrial economy, skills were stable and unchanging. Learning became a luxury for good times, cut when business is tight.
- **Buy rather than build talent**: The Tin Man default is "if you don't have the talent, just buy it" through external hires.
- **Fear of enabling departure**: The old adage worries that training employees means they might leave -- but modern organizations should worry that not training them means they'll stay.
- **Separation from work**: Learning is viewed as something to bestow when time and budget allow, outsourced to HR, which mechanically designs standardized training violating principles of andragogy (adult learning) -- self-direction, relevance, and problem-centered learning.
- **Unicorn job searches**: "Wanted: an individual with a GPA of 4.0 who has transformed at least two major Swiss-based health care multinationals using generative AI." Organizations search for unicorns instead of developing existing talent.
- **Unpredictable skill needs**: It's gotten harder to predict needed skills even six months out (how many people were ready to work with LLMs when they arrived?).

Organizations with more than 50 percent digitally fluent leaders outperform peers by 48 percent in revenue growth and valuation. Yet internal training integrated into jobs remains low.

## The Octopus Way

Octopus Organizations weave learning into the daily fabric of work. Like the octopus whose short lifespan forces it to integrate learning into everything it does, every project and interaction comes with learning.

**Key Octopus behaviors:**

- **Make learning the work.** Every project retrospective becomes a learning opportunity. Team meetings incorporate knowledge sharing. Status meetings and one-on-ones transform from bureaucratic chores to development dialogues.
- **Find inexpensive ways to learn.** At Tesco Bank, Jana created ninety-second videos on topics like the perils of multitasking -- zero-cost content any employee could replicate. Google's g2g program reports 80 percent of tracked training is employee-to-employee, with over six thousand volunteer teachers. Ferrari CEO Benedetto Vigna sends "management pills" -- doses of wisdom to stimulate curiosity.
- **Learn together.** Former Slack and Google executive Brian Elliott sends teams on training together: "The magic is not just that you put your hands on it, but you do it with a team." Datadog COO Adam Blitzer embeds individuals with other teams -- a simple reassignment that costs nothing but creates cross-pollination.
- **Close the forgetting gap.** 90 percent of new skills not used within a year after learning are forgotten. The gap between where training happens and its application must be minimized.
- **Require unlearning.** Alphabet X CEO Astro Teller: "I'm trying to train people to unlearn all the things that have been piled on top of them since they were six years old." Barry O'Reilly advocates a continuous cycle: unlearning outdated approaches, relearning through experimentation, achieving breakthroughs.
- **Map capabilities against future needs.** Create skills matrices showing current capabilities and future requirements. This informs strategic reskilling that proactively prevents costly talent scrambles.
- **Prioritize learning hunger over credentials.** Jessica Hall at Just Eat Takeaway lets hungry learners move through the organization, changing positions every eighteen months, improving retention.
- **Create career lattices, not ladders.** Webs of growth that move sideways, diagonally, or even temporarily down. These "boundary spanners" become connectors across silos.
- **Measure progress.** Werner Swanepoel at Sibanye-Stillwater uses a "digital dipstick" at every town hall measuring team excitement, self-rated skills, and ability to apply skills.

## Key Principles

1. **Learning is the work, not separate from it** -- embed it into daily flow, not as an HR-managed luxury
2. **Develop, don't acquire** -- investing in existing people is more sustainable than perpetual external hiring
3. **Unlearning is as important as learning** -- outdated mindsets are the greatest challenge
4. **Career lattices over career ladders** -- growth should move in all directions, creating boundary spanners
5. **Close the forgetting gap** -- skills must be practiced immediately or they atrophy

## Real-World Examples

- **Google g2g**: 80% of tracked training is employee-to-employee with 6,000+ volunteer teachers
- **Tesco Bank**: Jana's ninety-second DIY videos on practical topics -- zero cost
- **Ferrari**: CEO Vigna's "management pills" stimulating curiosity and learning
- **Datadog**: COO Adam Blitzer embeds individuals with other teams for cross-pollination at no cost
- **Just Eat Takeaway**: Jessica Hall's policy of moving learners every 18 months improves retention
- **Sibanye-Stillwater**: Werner Swanepoel's "digital dipstick" at town halls measures development progress
- **Alphabet X**: Astro Teller on unlearning childhood conditioning to seek correct answers and avoid mistakes
- **Airbus**: VP Fabrice Valentin on ownership coupled with practical impact accelerating learning

## Practical Takeaways

1. **Make learning the work** -- embed learning into initiatives via 2-3 day focused sprints; use Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle (Experience, Reflect, Conceptualize, Experiment)
2. **Create micro-learning content** -- shift from expensive omnibus programs to 3-minute videos, 5-minute quizzes, and 7-minute leadership podcasts, many developed by employees for employees
3. **Strategically plan skills development** -- proactively identify near-future skills, conduct regular skills-gap analyses, link skill acquisition to career progression
4. **Support metacognitive skill development** -- teach people how to learn: identify assumptions, create learning plans, use reflection journals, promote peer feedback emphasizing learning strategies
5. **Enable self-organized curiosity groups** -- encourage employee-created guilds around shared interest with no management required
6. **Create space for regular development conversations** -- equip leaders with questions like "What skill are you trying to build, and how can I help you get practice?"
7. **Create "skill swaps" marketplaces** -- "Airbnb for skills" where employees list skills they have and want to learn
8. **Set up a "curiosity budget"** -- a small budget for books, courses, conferences, or side projects with autonomy to choose
