# Antipattern #4: Pursuing Fluffy Goals

*Goals connected to customer impact guide growth.*

## The Antipattern

Organizations announce ambitious goals that are vague and lack clear connections to the organization's purpose and the ability to guide people's work. Teams struggle to know what to work toward, and projects lose momentum when teams cannot connect work to outcomes.

Richard Rumelt in *Good Strategy, Bad Strategy* calls these "bad strategic objectives" or "fluff" -- goals that stem from skipping the crucial step of identifying the core challenges the organization must overcome to execute its strategy.

Le-Brun and Werner identify five common types of fluffy goals:

1. **Ambition masking as strategic goals**: "Expand into 100 new cities by 2025" without diagnosis of obstacles or competitive landscape -- wishful thinking detached from reality
2. **Ambiguous goals**: "Improve customer satisfaction" without defining why, how, measurement, or magnitude
3. **Cosmetic goals**: Public responses to trends using jargon like "AI transformation" that mask the absence of a clear problem
4. **Incoherent or conflicting goals**: Wanting to "achieve market leadership" while simultaneously imposing aggressive cost-cutting targets
5. **Blue sky goals**: "Dominate the global enterprise market within three years" without any credible plan or unique capability

## Why It Happens

The authors describe a **predictable failure pattern** they have witnessed repeatedly:

1. A leader is brought in who has succeeded with an ambitious goal elsewhere. The failure rate for ill-defined ambitions led by external recruits exceeds 40%, with average tenure for roles like chief digital officers a mere 2.5 years. The very existence of the role subtly allows the rest of the C-suite to abdicate their responsibilities.
2. Planning follows, with tweaks as departments attempt to align vague goals with their own interests. Slick PowerPoints create false indication of momentum.
3. Experiments occur, often isolated from core business. Local success rarely permeates the broader organization due to lack of clear linkage to the vague overarching goal.
4. The **"oh shit" moment** -- time constraints force frenetic activity, teams compete for resources, dates become targets not valuable outcomes, failure looks increasingly certain.
5. The person brought in quietly exits.

**Consequences**: Resource allocation becomes political. Employees disengage. Energy is consumed trying to interpret and "align" under ambiguity. Projects fail. Investor skepticism. The opportunity cost of chasing mirages swells.

Root causes include: the machinery of goal setting (rigorous annual processes) that lacks the human judgment and strategic insight to translate choices into focused goals; and organizations that have vague strategies compounding the problem at the goal level.

In one week, three CEOs from three different industries shared similarly vague goals with the authors -- all variants of "We're going to double X in five years by doing Y." When asked why "double" and why "five years," they were bemused. When employees were asked about the consequence of these goals on their work, most stared blankly.

## The Octopus Way

**Diagnose before setting goals.** Instead of jumping to targets like "double revenue," Octopus Organizations dive deep to understand specific operational or market challenges and opportunities.

**Connect goals to the "why."** Frame through customer-centric causes. Develop a compelling rationale for why the goal benefits customers and stakeholders and why it matters to those executing it. The intrinsic why translates into a measurable what, articulated in simple language.

**Set specific, understandable, and public goals** that guide progress without prescriptively defining how to achieve them, allowing learning to happen. Jeff Bezos: "We are stubborn on vision but flexible on detail."

**Satya Nadella at Microsoft** exemplifies this. His clear diagnosis: the company needed to pivot to the cloud. His "cloud-first, mobile-first" strategy led to specific goals without prescribing how: increase cloud revenue to $20B by 2018; 75% of server products delivered as cloud services by 2020; double cloud enterprise customer base annually for three years.

**Back goals with coherent actions** -- specific, coordinated activities and resource commitments. Make the map transparent and a living document. **McDonald's 2017 "Velocity Growth Plan"**: CEO Steve Easterbrook diagnosed customer retention and growth as critical, set a guiding approach of rapid modernization and customer convenience, then set audacious short-term goals -- deploy e-commerce in 20,000 restaurants, launch home delivery in 8,000, reimagine customer experience in thousands more, all within a year. These coherent actions forced new, agile ways of working.

**Foster clear ownership for goals** with regular reviews (weekly or monthly check-ins) and feedback loops focused on current challenges, cross-functional support needs, and ensuring goals remain a living, collective responsibility. Dame Julia Hoggett at the London Stock Exchange practices weekly meetings bringing diverse players together to share updates and tackle issues collaboratively.

**Use goal frameworks thoughtfully.** OKRs (objectives and key results), originating at Intel under Andy Grove and introduced to Google by John Doerr, help focus on priorities, promote transparent cross-team alignment, and encourage stretch goals. But a warning: OKR implementations often become mechanistic exercises in goal setting and performance tracking, setting output-style metrics that do not get to the goal. As former Google Development Director Chris Butler highlights, OKRs must reflect genuine strategic priorities and drive desired outcomes, not just activity.

## Key Principles

- **Diagnosis before prescription** (Richard Rumelt): Rigorously understand the challenge before setting targets
- **"Stubborn on vision, flexible on detail"** (Jeff Bezos): Goals should guide without prescribing the path
- **Coherent action** (Rumelt): Goals must be backed by specific, coordinated activities and resource commitments
- **Input metrics over output metrics**: Focus on metrics teams can directly influence (customer contact frequency, experiment velocity, cycle time) rather than lagging output indicators
- **OKRs done right**: Objectives are qualitative (what you aim to achieve); key results are quantitative outcomes (not outputs or activities). Distinguish "committed KRs" (expected) from "aspirational KRs" (stretch).
- **Clarity enables motivation** (Daniel Pink): When people understand the purpose behind goals and how their work contributes to meaningful outcomes, they can buy in and deliver

## Real-World Examples

- **General Electric (2015)** -- Announced a plan to become a top ten software company within five years. But how? What kind of software? Why? Despite a $4B investment, the goal failed to gain traction.
- **Ford** -- Set the ambition to become a "mobility company"; derailed by vague goals
- **Microsoft** -- Satya Nadella's clear diagnosis (pivot to cloud) led to specific, measurable transformation goals: $20B cloud revenue by 2018, 75% cloud delivery by 2020, double enterprise customer base annually
- **McDonald's** -- 2017 "Velocity Growth Plan" under Steve Easterbrook: specific deployment targets (20,000 restaurants for e-commerce, 8,000 for home delivery) within one year, forcing new ways of working
- **London Stock Exchange** -- Dame Julia Hoggett's weekly cross-functional meetings to maintain goal ownership and tackle blockers collaboratively
- **Intel/Google** -- OKR framework origin (Andy Grove) and adoption (John Doerr introducing to Google in 1999)

## Practical Takeaways

1. **Diagnose before prescribing goals.** Ask: What is the most critical problem we must solve? What are the main obstacles? What are our unique capabilities or disadvantages?
2. **Define an approach and coherent actions based on diagnosis.** A small number of critical goals, each with specific coordinated activities, resource commitments, and changes to ways of working. Identify critical paths and cross-functional dependencies.
3. **Foster understanding and alignment.** Create a one-page narrative: why the goal matters to customers and purpose, how it delivers toward strategy, measurable outcomes, and high-level approach. Test with frontline teams and refine.
4. **Prioritize and ruthlessly sequence initiatives.** Make hard choices about which to pursue and which to forgo. Focus on near-term successes that validate the approach. Regularly conduct a "stop doing" review.
5. **Experiment with OKRs.** Translate priorities into qualitative objectives with quantitative key results measuring outcomes. Quarterly reviews with transparent communication. Train teams on OKR thinking (impact, learning), not just mechanics.
6. **Shift focus to controllable input metrics.** Identify metrics teams can directly influence that drive broader goals (e.g., customer contact frequency, experiment velocity, cycle time). These provide more immediate feedback than lagging output indicators.
