## The Antipattern

Tin Man organizations hire transactionally, driven by skill-laden job descriptions and immediate needs, while treating onboarding as a perfunctory administrative task. The results are a rigid workforce that struggles to adapt to evolving customer needs or capitalize on new technologies, increased turnover and recruitment waste, and amplification of existing skills gaps and weaknesses.

## Why It Happens

Organizations default to hiring for current technical skills rather than long-term potential because it feels safer and more immediate. Job descriptions become laundry lists of qualifications rather than outcome-focused documents. Hiring managers rush to fill positions under pressure, lowering quality standards for urgent needs. Once hired, new employees are dropped immediately into firefights and project work rather than being properly integrated into the culture and purpose of the organization. The result is a self-reinforcing cycle: poor hires lead to higher turnover, which creates more urgency to fill roles quickly, which leads to more poor hires.

## The Octopus Way

Octopus Organizations treat hiring as a critical strategic priority, prioritizing values alignment and learning agility over current skill fit. They invest in comprehensive, long-term onboarding programs and only hire when absolutely necessary.

**Raising the Bar with Every Hire.** Amazon expects new hires to be better than 50 percent of the individuals in that job and level. This is achieved through "bar raisers" -- skilled interviewers who ensure high standards are maintained. Bar raisers challenge hiring decisions, fight bias, and prevent lowering quality standards for urgent needs, having an equal say in the final decision as the hiring manager. As Jeff Bezos put it: "I'd rather interview fifty people and not hire anyone than hire the wrong person." Bar raisers drastically reduce turnover while increasing talent density.

**Investing Heavily in Onboarding.** Those first days in a new job are one of the most important inflection points for future productivity. Good onboarding establishes a unity of purpose rather than functional loyalty and directly impacts whether employees stay or leave -- and by extension, revenue. Onboarding sessions explain the organization's genesis, purpose, values, principles, strategy, and how it meets customer needs. Amazon's executive onboarding leader Will Thomas recommends extending onboarding to the full first year: "Good onboarding creates a sense of inclusion, and a desire to be part of the team."

**Accepting When Things Don't Work Out.** At Amazon, new hires can move internally one month after onboarding because they were hired for value fit but might not fit the specific team or role. Where things really are not working, Octopus Organizations help find a better fit rather than forcing a bad match.

## Key Principles

- Write outcome-based job descriptions connected to organizational purpose, replacing vague qualities with observable behaviors
- Screen for learning drive, adaptability, and experimental mindset -- not just credentials
- Let teams build teams through collaborative interviews and team hiring
- Embed social elements into hiring (team lunches, coffee chats, collaborative work sessions)
- Test bar raisers as cultural guardians with meaningful veto power
- Create individualized onboarding plans of at least three months (ideally a full year)
- Close the loop with exit interviews, onboarding assessments, and engagement surveys

## Real-World Examples

- **Amazon's Bar Raiser Program**: Trained interviewers who embody organizational values evaluate candidates independently using behavioral techniques, with veto power over hiring decisions
- **Amazon's Internal Mobility**: New hires can transfer teams after just one month of onboarding, recognizing that value fit does not always equal team fit
- **Will Thomas's Onboarding Philosophy**: Amazon's executive onboarding leader advocates for a full-year onboarding process including three-month check-ins with hiring managers as "a demonstration of care"

## Practical Takeaways

1. Replace skill-focused job descriptions with outcome-based ones that connect roles to expected impact
2. Add interview questions that reveal learning orientation: "How do you decide how to hit goals?" (experimental mindset), "What constitutes a bad day for you?" (resilience), "What kinds of people do you work best with?" (adaptability)
3. Assign learning buddies to new hires to improve performance, retention, commitment, and competence
4. Protect onboarding time organizationally -- set an expectation that new recruits will not be dropped into project work immediately
5. Report on onboarding progress to ensure it is prioritized, not sidelined
