## The Antipattern

Tin Man organizations rely on inadequate proxies -- internal voices, assumptions, past experiences, and easy-to-implement metrics -- to avoid the messy work of engaging directly with actual customers. The results are missed opportunities to meet unidentified customer needs, increased irrelevance to evolving customers, and wasted resources on mismatched products and services.

## Why It Happens

**Success breeds complacency.** Teams stop listening, especially to those who "quietly send us their money without complaint." As entrepreneur Barry O'Reilly observes, organizations take loyal customers for granted, forgetting how to excite them and turn them into fans.

**Arrogance.** Organizations believe they know what customers want because they have succeeded before. One COO told the authors his successful firm had a "divine right to exist." A distinguished engineer with a PhD blamed customers for being "stupid" when his product flopped.

**Proxy measures create false images.** Net Promoter Score (NPS) misses signals -- it does not capture mildly happy customers who will jump to a competitor for slightly better price or attention. Worse proxies send opposite signals: a call center's declining call volume is treated as satisfaction when it may mean more customers giving up. Chief Customer Officers become overrelied on as the one Voice of the Customer instead of actual customer engagement.

**Weak signals are ignored.** Limited opt-in surveys give a false sense of security while frontline workers who see customers daily notice changes that need addressing.

Phil Le-Brun experienced this firsthand as McDonald's international CIO. Between 2012 and 2017, McDonald's lost roughly 500 million customer transactions (a year-on-year drop of 1.7 percent). Decisions were being made with the question "What would the franchisees think?" -- all-day breakfast was too operationally complex for franchisees; home delivery too expensive. The real customer had been forgotten.

Jeff Bezos's 2016 shareholder letter coined the antidote to this: "Day 2" -- "Stasis. Followed by irrelevance. Followed by excruciating, painful decline. Followed by death."

## The Octopus Way

**Customer Obsession by Design.** Octopus Organizations are designed, like the real octopus from neural clusters to tentacles, to be constantly sensing and responding to the customer environment. At Amazon, an empty chair is often present in meetings as a reminder of the customer. At Tesco headquarters, public spaces are covered with customer photos and meeting rooms designed like typical customer homes.

**Direct Customer Engagement.** A US hardware retailer's digital design teams went into their own stores to buy custom paint and chatted with customers before building an online version. Lego develops fans' ideas into official products. Salesforce Ventures' Innovation Advisory Board is composed of prominent customers. Phil Le-Brun spent days working in McDonald's restaurants; Jana Werner listened to insurance customer calls, used her own company's banking products, and stacked shelves at Christmas.

**Unfiltered Feedback.** T-Mobile CEO John Legere installed a phone line in his office to listen directly to customer service calls for three hours a day, leading directly to the company's first prepaid product. He replies personally to customer messages. PepsiCo CEO Indra Nooyi visited restaurants and rode with delivery drivers.

**Working Backwards (PR-FAQ).** Amazon's mechanism requires teams to write a press release and FAQ as if their product had already launched, forcing customer-centric thinking. Teams go through multiple drafts with extensive customer research. The process forces uncomfortable questions: Are we solving a real problem? Can we articulate value in terms customers care about?

**Productive Paranoia.** Octopus Organizations assume customers are always changing and that switching costs are low enough that any customer will bail for a slightly better situation. Amazon's heartbeat tool aggregates near-real-time data from more than a dozen sources for unfiltered customer insights.

**Anecdotes Matter.** Customer anecdotes -- a tweet, a survey comment, a call center conversation -- are documented, studied, and used to inform strategy. These are often discarded as outliers but may indicate significant emerging issues missed in statistics.

**Minimum Lovable Products (MLPs).** Jeff Wilkie (Amazon's former CEO Worldwide Consumer) found that MVPs prioritized speed over customer delight. MLPs create something with just enough features for early customers to love the experience, which tends to make products climb the adoption curve rapidly.

## Key Principles

- Customer value is the purpose of the organization -- the driving force behind every decision, innovation, and interaction
- Proxies are easier than engaging with customers, but you get what you pay for
- The cost of poor service and lack of customer-centricity is $1.9 trillion in the United States alone
- Reconnecting with customers is not mastering a complex new art -- it is reawakening innate curiosity and making direct engagement a daily practice
- Talk about products from the customer perspective: not "implementing a claims processing system" but "helping families get back on their feet faster after disasters"

## Real-World Examples

- **McDonald's (Phil Le-Brun)**: Lost 500 million customer transactions by focusing on franchisee needs over customer needs
- **Amazon**: Empty chair in meetings, Working Backwards PR-FAQ, heartbeat tool, Day 1/Day 2 philosophy
- **T-Mobile (John Legere)**: Three hours daily listening to customer service calls, leading to first prepaid product
- **PepsiCo (Indra Nooyi)**: Visited restaurants and rode with delivery drivers for frontline feedback
- **Tesco**: Headquarters designed with customer photos and meeting rooms resembling customer homes
- **Lego**: Develops fans' ideas into official products
- **Microsoft (Satya Nadella)**: Takes two calls a day from other companies' CEOs
- **Procter & Gamble**: Sends product teams to live with customers for days
- **Microsoft Xbox**: Transformed strategy after discovering through home visits that consoles were becoming family entertainment hubs
- **Jeff Wilkie / Amazon**: Shifted from MVPs to MLPs (Minimum Lovable Products)

## Practical Takeaways

1. Reduce distance between customers and leadership: require senior leaders to engage directly through service lines, feedback review sessions, and industry events
2. Keep customers visible: empty chairs in meetings, customer photos in office spaces, public displays of customer data
3. Create customer opportunity statements: "[Customer type] currently faces [specific problem] when [situation occurs]"
4. Define customers using detailed personas with names, stories, and specific scenarios ("Meet Lorraine, the Busy, On-the-Go Consumer")
5. Dive deep into research early: user research, empathy mapping, funnel analysis, customer reviews, social media mentions
6. Change the language: talk about products from the customer perspective
7. Incentivize customer adoption, not project completion -- McDonald's developers were compensated for building systems customers adopted
8. Live with your customers as Procter & Gamble does
